<SlideLayout title="The Gaussian integers are Euclidean">

The Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i] = \{a + bi \mid a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ form a Euclidean domain with norm $N(a + bi) = a^2 + b^2$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Division in ℤ[i]">
Given $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$ with $\beta \neq 0$, form the complex number $\alpha/\beta \in \mathbb{C}$. Round each coordinate to the nearest integer to get $q \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$, and set $r = \alpha - q\beta$.

The key estimate: rounding a complex number to the nearest Gaussian integer moves it by at most $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} < 1$. So
$$N(r) = N(\alpha/\beta - q) \cdot N(\beta) \leq \frac{1}{2} N(\beta) < N(\beta).$$
</Callout>

The same trick works for the **Eisenstein integers** $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ where $\omega = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{3}i}{2}$ (a primitive cube root of unity): the Eisenstein lattice is dense enough that rounding works similarly.

<Callout kind="remark">
The ring $\mathbb{Z}[\frac{1+\sqrt{-19}}{2}]$ is a PID but **not** Euclidean — no Euclidean norm exists. So the inclusion Euclidean $\subsetneq$ PID is strict.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

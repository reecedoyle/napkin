<SlideLayout title="Every PID is a UFD">

The following theorem places PIDs and UFDs in a clean hierarchy.

<Callout kind="theorem">
Let $R$ be a PID. Then $R$ is a UFD.
</Callout>

We won't prove this in full here, but the key ideas are:

1. **Existence of factorisation:** In a PID, every ascending chain of ideals eventually stabilises (the "Noetherian" property), which forces every element to eventually factor into irreducibles.
2. **Uniqueness:** In a PID, irreducible elements are the same as prime elements (meaning $p \mid ab \Rightarrow p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$). Uniqueness of factorisation follows from primality.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The hierarchy">
$$\text{Fields} \subsetneq \text{Euclidean domains} \subsetneq \text{PIDs} \subsetneq \text{UFDs} \subsetneq \text{Integral domains} \subsetneq \text{Rings}$$
Each inclusion is strict — there are PIDs that are not Euclidean, UFDs that are not PIDs, etc.
</Callout>

The failure of $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ to be a UFD reflects the fact that it's also not a PID: the ideal $(2, 1 + \sqrt{-5})$ is not principal.

</SlideLayout>

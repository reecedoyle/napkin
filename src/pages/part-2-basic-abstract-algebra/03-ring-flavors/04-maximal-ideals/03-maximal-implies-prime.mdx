<SlideLayout title="Maximal implies prime" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Maximal ideals">

The two notions are related by a clean implication:

<Callout kind="corollary" title="Every maximal ideal is prime">
If $I$ is a maximal ideal of a ring $R$, then $I$ is prime.
</Callout>

**Proof:** $I$ maximal $\Rightarrow$ $R/I$ is a field $\Rightarrow$ $R/I$ is an integral domain $\Rightarrow$ $I$ is prime.

The converse fails: $(x) \subseteq \mathbb{Z}[x]$ is prime (quotient is $\mathbb{Z}$, an integral domain) but not maximal (quotient is $\mathbb{Z}$, not a field).

<Callout kind="remark" title="When does prime = maximal?">
In $\mathbb{Z}$, every nonzero prime ideal is also maximal — the nonzero prime ideals are exactly $(p)$ for prime $p$, and $\mathbb{Z}/(p) = \mathbb{F}_p$ is a field. This special property (every nonzero prime ideal is maximal) characterises **Dedekind domains**, where unique factorisation of ideals holds. More on that later.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

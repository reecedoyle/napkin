<SlideLayout title="Using quotients to check maximality" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Maximal ideals">

The quotient theorems turn maximality checks into field checks — which is usually much easier.

<Callout kind="example" title="Modding out in ℤ[x]">
- **$(x, 5) \subseteq \mathbb{Z}[x]$ is maximal.** Modding out by $x$ kills the polynomial variable, giving $\mathbb{Z}$. Then modding out by $5$ gives $\mathbb{F}_5$. So $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x, 5) \cong \mathbb{F}_5$, a field.
- **$(x) \subseteq \mathbb{Z}[x]$ is prime but not maximal.** $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, which is an integral domain (so prime) but not a field (so not maximal).
</Callout>

This pattern generalises: to test whether a generated ideal is maximal or prime, compute what the quotient ring looks like and ask whether it's a field or just an integral domain.

The technique of "mod out and identify the quotient" is one of the most frequently used methods in ring theory. Keep it in your toolkit.

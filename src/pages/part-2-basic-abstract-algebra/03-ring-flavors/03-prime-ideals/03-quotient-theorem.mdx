There's a clean characterisation of prime ideals in terms of quotient rings — and it's essentially just definition chasing.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Prime ideal ⟺ quotient is an integral domain">
An ideal $I \subsetneq R$ is prime if and only if the quotient ring <Term k="quotientRing">$R/I$</Term> is an integral domain.
</Callout>

**Why this works:** The condition "$xy \in I \Rightarrow x \in I$ or $y \in I$" translates directly to "$\bar x \bar y = \bar 0 \Rightarrow \bar x = \bar 0$ or $\bar y = \bar 0$" in $R/I$ — which is exactly saying $R/I$ has no zero divisors.

This gives us a fast way to check primality:

- $\mathbb{Z}/(7) \cong \mathbb{Z}/7\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{F}_7$, a field (hence integral domain), so $(7)$ is prime. ✓
- $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, an integral domain, so $(x)$ is prime. ✓
- $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(3, x) \cong \mathbb{F}_3$, a field, so $(3, x)$ is prime. ✓

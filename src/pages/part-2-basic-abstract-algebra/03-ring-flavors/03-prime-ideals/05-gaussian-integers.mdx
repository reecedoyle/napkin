<SlideLayout title="A prime ideal that is not prime as an element — ℤ[i]" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Prime ideals">

The element-versus-ideal distinction matters. In $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (the Gaussian integers), the element $5$ is a perfectly good prime in $\mathbb{Z}$, but in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ it splits:

$$5 = (2 + i)(2 - i)$$

so $5$ is *not* irreducible in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$.

Consequently, the ideal $(5) \subseteq \mathbb{Z}[i]$ is **not** prime:

$$3 + i \text{ and } 3 - i \text{ multiply to } (3+i)(3-i) = 10 \in (5)$$

yet neither $3 + i$ nor $3 - i$ lies in $(5)$.

<Callout kind="remark">
This illustrates why the passage from integers to general rings requires working with ideals rather than elements. The ideal-theoretic approach recovers unique factorisation in rings like $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ — but via ideals, not element factorisation.
</Callout>

We will revisit Gaussian integers properly in the algebraic number theory chapters. For now, they serve as a warning: even familiar-seeming rings can behave differently.

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="Principal ideal domains">

Among integral domains, there's a particularly well-behaved subclass where every ideal is generated by a single element — a "principal" ideal.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Principal ideal domain (PID)">
An integral domain in which every ideal is principal is called a <Term k="pid">**principal ideal domain (PID)**</Term>.
</Callout>

Recall that the ideal $(a, b)$ is the ring-analogue of $\gcd(a, b)$. So what a PID really says is:

<Callout kind="remark" title="The moral of a PID">
In a PID, you can take the $\gcd$ of any collection of elements. The ideal generated by any family $\{a_i\}$ is in fact generated by a single element — their "greatest common divisor".
</Callout>

**Examples:**
- $\mathbb{Z}$ is a PID. The ideal $(a, b) = (\gcd(a, b))$ — this is essentially Bézout's lemma.
- $\mathbb{R}[x]$, $\mathbb{Q}[x]$: polynomial rings over a field are PIDs.
- $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is a *principal ideal ring* (every ideal is principal) but is **not** an integral domain, so it's not a PID.
- $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is **not** a PID — the ideal $(2, x)$ is not principal.

</SlideLayout>

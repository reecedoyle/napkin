<SlideLayout title="Integral domains — definition" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Integral domains">

<Callout kind="definition" title="Integral domain">
A nontrivial ring with no zero divisors is called an <Term k="integralDomain">**integral domain**</Term>.
</Callout>

Equivalently: $R$ is an integral domain if $R \neq 0$ and whenever $ab = 0$ in $R$, then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$.

Every field is an integral domain: if $ab = 0$ and $b \neq 0$, multiply by $b^{-1}$ to get $a = 0$. But integral domains need not be fields — $\mathbb{Z}$ has no zero divisors yet $2$ has no inverse.

<Callout kind="example" title="Examples of integral domains">
- $\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain (but not a field).
- $\mathbb{R}[x]$: if $P(x)Q(x) = 0$ then one of $P$, $Q$ is the zero polynomial. (Integral domain, not a field.)
- $\mathbb{Z}[x]$: same argument. In fact $R[x]$ is an integral domain whenever $R$ is.
- $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is an integral domain (equivalently, a field) exactly when $n$ is prime.
- The trivial ring $\{0\}$ is **not** considered an integral domain.
</Callout>

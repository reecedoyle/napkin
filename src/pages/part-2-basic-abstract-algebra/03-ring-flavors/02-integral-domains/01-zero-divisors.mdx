<SlideLayout title="Zero divisors">

In a field we can divide — if $ab = 0$ and $b \neq 0$, multiply through by $b^{-1}$ to get $a = 0$. So fields have the **zero product property**: a product is zero only when at least one factor is zero.

Many rings we care about — <Term k="ZZ">$\mathbb{Z}$</Term>, $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $\mathbb{R}[x]$ — share this property even though they aren't fields.

But not all rings do. In $\mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}$:
$$3 \cdot 5 \equiv 0 \pmod{15}$$
even though neither $3$ nor $5$ is zero.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Zero divisor">
In a ring $R$, a nonzero element $a$ is a <Term k="zeroDivisor">**zero divisor**</Term> if there exists a nonzero $b \in R$ with $ab = 0$.
</Callout>

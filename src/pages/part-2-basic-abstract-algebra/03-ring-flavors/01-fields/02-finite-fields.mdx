<SlideLayout title="Finite fields" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Fields">

Fields don't have to be infinite. The classic finite example is <Term k="Zc">$\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$</Term> for a prime $p$ — clock arithmetic mod $p$.

<Callout kind="example" title="𝔽_p — the field with p elements">
For a prime $p$, the ring $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is a field. Every nonzero element $\bar a$ has a multiplicative inverse: since $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, Bézout's lemma gives integers $u, v$ with $au + pv = 1$, so $\bar a \cdot \bar u = \bar 1$ in $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$.

We write <Term k="finiteField">$\mathbb{F}_p$</Term> for this field.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark">
When $n$ is composite, $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is **not** a field. For example, in $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, the element $\bar 2$ has no inverse: $2 \cdot 1 = 2$, $2 \cdot 2 = 4$, $2 \cdot 3 = 0$, $2 \cdot 4 = 2$, $2 \cdot 5 = 4$ — none equal $1$.
</Callout>

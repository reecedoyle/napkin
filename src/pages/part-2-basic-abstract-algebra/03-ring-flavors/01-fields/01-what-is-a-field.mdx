<SlideLayout title="What is a field?" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="5. Flavors of rings · Fields">

A **ring** already gives us addition and multiplication. A **field** asks for one more thing: every nonzero element must be invertible under multiplication — you can always divide.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Field">
A **field** is a nontrivial ring <Term k="field">$K$</Term> in which every nonzero element is a unit (has a multiplicative inverse).
</Callout>

Concretely: $K$ is a field exactly when for every $a \in K$ with $a \neq 0$, there exists $b \in K$ with $ab = 1$.

The prototype is <Term k="QQ">$\mathbb{Q}$</Term>. Every nonzero rational has a reciprocal. Compare with <Term k="ZZ">$\mathbb{Z}$</Term>: the integer $2$ has no multiplicative inverse inside $\mathbb{Z}$, so $\mathbb{Z}$ is a ring but not a field.

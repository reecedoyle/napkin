<SlideLayout title="Polynomial rings" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="4. Rings and ideals · Definition and examples">

Given any ring $R$, we can build a new one by adjoining a formal variable $x$.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Polynomial ring R[x]">
The **polynomial ring** <Term k="polyRing">$R[x]$</Term> consists of all polynomials with coefficients in $R$:

$$R[x] = \bigl\{\, a_n x^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0 \;\big|\; a_0,\dots,a_n \in R \,\bigr\}.$$

Addition and multiplication are the familiar polynomial operations. Pronounced "$R$ adjoin $x$".
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Polynomial division caveat">
If $p \in R[x]$ and $p(a) = 0$, then $(x - a)$ divides $p(x)$ — as expected. But pathologies arise without zero-divisor assumptions. For instance, $x^2 - 1 \equiv (x-1)(x+1) \pmod{8}$ has **four** roots in $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$: $1, 3, 5, 7$. The culprit: $2 \cdot 4 = 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$. In an **integral domain** (a ring without zero divisors), this pathology disappears.
</Callout>

We can also adjoin multiple variables: $R[x_1, \dots, x_n]$, or even infinitely many.

</SlideLayout>

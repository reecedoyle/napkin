<SlideLayout title="The prototype ring is ℤ">

<Term k="ZZ">$\mathbb{Z}$</Term> is the ring to keep in mind at all times. Everything we do is a generalisation of familiar integer arithmetic.

<Callout kind="example" title="Why ℤ is the model">
- "Irreducible polynomial in <Term k="QQ">$\mathbb{Q}$</Term><Term k="polyRing">$[x]$</Term>" works exactly like "prime in $\mathbb{Z}$".
- "Factoring polynomials mod $p$" mirrors "unique factorisation in $\mathbb{Z}$".
- Even Fermat's Last Theorem ($x^n + y^n = z^n$) requires stepping outside $\mathbb{Z}$ into rings like $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_p]$.
</Callout>

A secondary source of examples is algebraic geometry: rings of polynomial functions on geometric spaces. That connection will be developed in a later part of the book.

</SlideLayout>

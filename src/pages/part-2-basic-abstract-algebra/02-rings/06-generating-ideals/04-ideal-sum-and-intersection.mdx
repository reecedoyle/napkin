<SlideLayout title="Sums and intersections of ideals">

Given two ideals $I$ and $J$ of a ring $R$, we can form new ideals from them.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Sum and intersection of ideals">
- The **sum** $I + J = \{\, a + b \mid a \in I, b \in J \,\}$ is an ideal — the smallest ideal containing both $I$ and $J$.
- The **intersection** $I \cap J$ is an ideal — the largest ideal contained in both.
- The **product** $IJ$ is the ideal generated by $\{ab : a \in I, b \in J\}$ — contained in $I \cap J$ but potentially smaller.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="In ℤ">
For $I = (m)$ and $J = (n)$ in $\mathbb{Z}$:

- $I + J = (\gcd(m, n))$ — by Bézout's identity.
- $I \cap J = (\text{lcm}(m, n))$ — divisible by both means divisible by lcm.
- $IJ = (mn)$ — products of multiples of $m$ and $n$.

For $m = 6, n = 10$: $I + J = (2)$, $I \cap J = (30)$, $IJ = (60)$.
</Callout>

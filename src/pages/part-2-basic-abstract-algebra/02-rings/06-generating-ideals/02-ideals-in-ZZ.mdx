<SlideLayout title="Ideals in ℤ" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="4. Rings and ideals · Generating ideals">

Every ideal of <Term k="ZZ">$\mathbb{Z}$</Term> has the form $n\mathbb{Z}$ for some $n \geq 0$.

This means every ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$ is **principal**: generated by a single element $n$, written <Term k="principalIdeal">$(n)$</Term>.

<Callout kind="example" title="GCD via ideals in ℤ">
In $\mathbb{Z}$, the ideal $(10, 15) = \{10x + 15y \mid x, y \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ equals $5\mathbb{Z} = (5)$.

This is Bézout's identity in disguise: $\gcd(10, 15) = 5$, and $(a, b) = \gcd(a,b)\mathbb{Z}$ in $\mathbb{Z}$.

This is why the GCD of two numbers is often written $(a, b)$ in number theory — it literally is the ideal generated by $a$ and $b$.
</Callout>

The pattern: in rings where every ideal is principal, there is always a notion of "greatest common divisor". This motivates the next section.

<Callout kind="example" title="The standard fields">
- <Term k="QQ">$\mathbb{Q}$</Term>, <Term k="RR">$\mathbb{R}$</Term>, <Term k="CC">$\mathbb{C}$</Term> are fields — every nonzero element has a reciprocal.
- For a prime $p$, the ring $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is a field, denoted <Term k="finiteField">$\mathbb{F}_p$</Term>. Primality is essential: if $p$ is not prime, there are zero divisors.
- The zero ring is **not** a field (we require nontriviality).
</Callout>

Why does primality make $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ a field? In $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$, every nonzero residue $\bar{a}$ satisfies $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, so Bézout's theorem gives $ax + py = 1$ for some $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}$, meaning $\bar{a} \cdot \bar{x} = \bar{1}$.

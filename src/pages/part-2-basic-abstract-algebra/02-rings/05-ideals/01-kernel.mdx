<SlideLayout title="The kernel of a ring homomorphism">

Just as with groups, ring homomorphisms have kernels — and the kernels characterise the "normal sub-objects".

<Callout kind="definition" title="Kernel">
The **kernel** of a ring homomorphism $\phi \colon R \to S$ is

$$\ker \phi = \{\, r \in R \mid \phi(r) = 0_S \,\}.$$
</Callout>

Two obvious properties of any kernel:

1. **Closed under addition.** If $\phi(x) = \phi(y) = 0$, then $\phi(x + y) = 0$.
2. **Absorbs all multiplication.** If $\phi(x) = 0$ and $r \in R$ is arbitrary, then $\phi(rx) = \phi(r)\phi(x) = 0$. So $rx \in \ker \phi$ even though $r$ may not be.

These two properties define an **ideal** — the ring-theoretic analogue of a normal subgroup.

<SlideLayout title="Ideals — definition" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="4. Rings and ideals · Ideals">

<Callout kind="definition" title="Ideal">
A nonempty subset <Term k="ideal">$I \subseteq R$</Term> is an **ideal** if:

1. $I$ is closed under addition: $x, y \in I \Rightarrow x + y \in I$.
2. $I$ absorbs multiplication from $R$: $x \in I, r \in R \Rightarrow rx \in I$.

The ideal is **proper** if $I \neq R$.
</Callout>

The key subtlety in condition 2: $r$ ranges over **all** of $R$, not just elements of $I$. This is strictly stronger than $I$ being closed under its own multiplication.

<Callout kind="example" title="Prototypical ideal: 5ℤ ⊆ ℤ">
$I = 5\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -10, -5, 0, 5, 10, \ldots\}$ is an ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$. It is the kernel of the "reduce mod 5" homomorphism $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}$. It clearly closes under addition, and $15 \in I$, $999 \in \mathbb{Z}$ gives $15 \cdot 999 = 14985 \in I$ — absorbed.
</Callout>

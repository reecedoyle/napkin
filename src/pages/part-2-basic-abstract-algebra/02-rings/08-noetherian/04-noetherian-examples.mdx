<SlideLayout title="Which rings are Noetherian?">

<Callout kind="example" title="Noetherian rings">
- **Fields** are Noetherian: the only ideals are $(0)$ and $K$, both finitely generated.
- **PIDs** are Noetherian: every ideal is generated by one element.
- $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $\mathbb{Z}[x,y]$, $\mathbb{Q}[x_1,\dots,x_n]$: all Noetherian by the Hilbert basis theorem.
- Any quotient $R/I$ of a Noetherian ring $R$ is Noetherian.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Non-Noetherian ring">
$\mathbb{Z}[x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots]$ with infinitely many variables is **not** Noetherian. The ideal $(x_1, x_2, \dots)$ cannot be generated by a finite set, and the chain $(x_1) \subsetneq (x_1,x_2) \subsetneq \cdots$ never stabilises.
</Callout>

The slogan: "most rings you encounter in practice are Noetherian". Non-Noetherian rings arise when you allow infinitely many generators — a pathology that rarely appears in algebraic geometry or number theory.

</SlideLayout>

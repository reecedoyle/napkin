<SlideLayout title="Hilbert basis theorem" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="4. Rings and ideals · Noetherian rings">

$\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is not a PID, but is it Noetherian? The answer is a resounding yes.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Hilbert basis theorem">
If $R$ is a <Term k="noetherian">Noetherian</Term> ring, then $R[x]$ is also Noetherian.

By induction, $R[x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n]$ is Noetherian for any finite $n$.
</Callout>

Since $\mathbb{Z}$ is Noetherian (it is a PID), the theorem gives: $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $\mathbb{Z}[x,y]$, $\mathbb{Z}[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ are all Noetherian, even though only the first is a PID.

The proof is an olympiad-flavoured argument: given any ideal $I \subseteq R[x]$, construct a finite generating set by a careful leading-coefficient chase. It appears as a problem at the end of this chapter.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why Noetherian matters">
Noetherian rings are the natural setting for algebraic geometry: rings of polynomial functions on varieties. The Noetherian condition guarantees that geometric objects can be described by finitely many equations — a fundamental finiteness property. Most rings encountered in practice are Noetherian.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Two equivalent definitions of Noetherian">
For a ring $R$, the following are equivalent:

**(a)** Every ideal of $R$ is finitely generated.

**(b) Ascending chain condition (ACC):** there is no infinite strictly ascending chain of ideals $I_1 \subsetneq I_2 \subsetneq I_3 \subsetneq \cdots$.

A ring satisfying either (and hence both) condition is called <Term k="noetherian">**Noetherian**</Term>.
</Callout>

The equivalence is intuitive: from an infinitely generated ideal you build an ascending chain by adding one generator at a time. Conversely, a chain $I_1 \subsetneq I_2 \subsetneq \cdots$ gives an ideal $\bigcup I_n$ that needs infinitely many generators.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Who is Noetherian?">
Every field is Noetherian (only two ideals). Every PID is Noetherian (every ideal is finitely generated — in fact by one element). So the interesting question is whether rings like $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ are Noetherian — and they are, by the Hilbert basis theorem.
</Callout>

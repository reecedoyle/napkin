<SlideLayout title="PIDs and GCDs">

In a <Term k="pid">PID</Term>, there is always a meaningful notion of "greatest common divisor".

<Callout kind="proposition" title="GCDs in a PID">
In a PID $R$, for any $a, b \in R$, the ideal $(a) + (b)$ is principal — say $(a) + (b) = (d)$. This element $d$ is a **greatest common divisor** of $a$ and $b$: $d$ divides both $a$ and $b$, and any common divisor of $a$ and $b$ divides $d$.
</Callout>

This is Bézout's identity at the ring-theoretic level: in a PID, $d = \gcd(a, b)$ satisfies $d = ra + sb$ for some $r, s \in R$.

<Callout kind="example" title="GCD in k[x]">
In $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ (a PID), the ideal $(x^2 - 1) + (x - 1) = (x - 1)$, since $x - 1$ divides $x^2 - 1 = (x-1)(x+1)$. So $\gcd(x^2 - 1, x - 1) = x - 1$ in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$.
</Callout>

For rings that are not PIDs — like $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ — this ideal-GCD approach breaks down: $(x, 2)$ is not principal, so there is no single "gcd" of $x$ and $2$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$.

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="A non-principal ideal" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="4. Rings and ideals · Principal ideal domains">

Not every ideal is generated by a single element. Here is the canonical example.

<Callout kind="example" title="A non-principal ideal in ℤ[x]">
In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, the ideal $I = (x, 2015)$ — polynomials whose constant term is divisible by $2015$ — is **not** principal.

**Proof.** Suppose $I = (f)$ for some $f \in \mathbb{Z}[x]$. Then $f$ divides both $x$ and $2015$. The only polynomials dividing $x$ are constants $\pm 1$ and $\pm x$; the only polynomials dividing $2015$ are constants. So $f$ must be a unit $\pm 1$, making $I = \mathbb{Z}[x]$. But $1 \notin I$ (the constant $1$ is not divisible by $2015$). Contradiction.
</Callout>

So $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ has ideals that require two generators. Rings where every ideal needs only one generator are particularly well-behaved.

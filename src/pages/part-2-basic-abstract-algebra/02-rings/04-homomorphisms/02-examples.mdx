<SlideLayout title="Ring homomorphism examples">

<Callout kind="example" title="Homomorphisms">
- The identity map $R \to R$ is always a homomorphism.
- The reduction map $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}$, $n \mapsto n \bmod 5$, is a homomorphism.
- Evaluation at zero: $\mathbb{R}[x] \to \mathbb{R}$ by $p(x) \mapsto p(0)$ — takes the constant term.
- For any ring $R$, the unique map $R \to 0$ to the zero ring is a homomorphism.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Non-examples — the identity condition bites">
- $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$, $x \mapsto 2x$: fails both (2) and (3) — sends $1$ to $2$.
- If $S$ is nontrivial, the zero map $R \to S$, $x \mapsto 0$, fails (3): $\phi(1_R) = 0 \neq 1_S$.
- There is **no** ring homomorphism $\mathbb{Z}/2016\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$ at all.

Contrast with groups: there is always a trivial group homomorphism $G \to H$ (send everything to $1_H$). For rings, no such escape exists.
</Callout>

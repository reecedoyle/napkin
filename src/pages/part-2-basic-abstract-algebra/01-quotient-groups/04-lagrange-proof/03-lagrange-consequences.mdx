<SlideLayout title="Consequences of Lagrange's theorem" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · (Optional) Proof of Lagrange's theorem">

Lagrange's theorem has immediate structural consequences.

<Callout kind="corollary" title="Groups of prime order are cyclic">
If $|G| = p$ is prime, then $G$ has no subgroups other than $\{1\}$ and $G$ itself. In particular, $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ (cyclic of order $p$).
</Callout>

**Proof:** Any subgroup $H \leq G$ has $|H| \mid p$, so $|H| = 1$ or $|H| = p$. These give $H = \{1\}$ or $H = G$.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Converse is false">
Lagrange's converse fails: a group of order $12$ does not necessarily have a subgroup of order $6$ (though it does for abelian groups). The alternating group $A_4$ (order 12) has no subgroup of order 6.
</Callout>

<SlideLayout title="Presentations can look very different">

A group can have many presentations. The dihedral group $D_{2n}$ can be presented in at least two ways:

$$
D_{2n} = \langle r, s \mid r^n = s^2 = 1,\; rs = sr^{-1} \rangle
$$

$$
D_{2n} = \langle x, y \mid x^2 = y^2 = 1,\; (xy)^n = 1 \rangle.
$$

(To see they define the same group: set $x = s$ and $y = rs$ in the second.)

<Callout kind="remark" title="Presentations are hard to work with">
Determining whether two elements in a presentation are equal is, in general, **undecidable** — there is no algorithm that always terminates with an answer. Even determining whether a presented group is finite is undecidable. Presentations are compact to write but hard to reason about mechanically.
</Callout>

Specifying a homomorphism from a presented group is easy though: just say where each generator goes, and **check the relations hold** at the target.

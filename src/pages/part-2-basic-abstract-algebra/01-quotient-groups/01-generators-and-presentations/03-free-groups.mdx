<SlideLayout title="Free groups" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Generators and group presentations">

What is the "largest" group on $n$ generators — with no constraints at all?

<Callout kind="definition" title="Free group">
The <Term k="freeGroup">**free group on $n$ generators**</Term>, written $F_n$, has presentation

$$
F_n = \langle x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \rangle
$$

with **no relations**. Elements of $F_n$ are all finite strings (words) formed by appending copies of $x_i$ and $x_i^{-1}$, with adjacent $x_i x_i^{-1}$ cancelled.
</Callout>

For example, $F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle$ contains strings like $a$, $b^{-1}$, $aba^{-1}b^{-2}$, and infinitely many others — with no identification unless forced by cancellation alone.

<Callout kind="remark" title="F₁ ≅ ℤ">
The free group on one generator is just $\mathbb{Z}$: the only strings are $a^n$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, and concatenation is addition of exponents.
</Callout>

Every group with $n$ generators is a **quotient** of $F_n$: the generator names map across, and the relations become the "extra identifications" we mod out by. This is the link between presentations and quotient groups, which we develop in the next section.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Notation clash">
The notation $\langle a, b \rangle$ can mean either the free group $F_2$ or the subgroup of some ambient group generated by $a$ and $b$. Context distinguishes them.
</Callout>

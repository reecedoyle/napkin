<SlideLayout title="Generators in finite groups" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Generators and group presentations">

In an **infinite** group, the "and their inverses" clause in the definition of $\langle S \rangle$ is essential: without $-1$, the element $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$ generates only $\{0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}$, not all of $\mathbb{Z}$.

In a **finite** group this concern disappears.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Inverses are redundant for finite groups">
If $G$ is finite and $S \subseteq G$, then $\langle S \rangle$ equals the set of all finite products of elements of $S$ (inverses not needed separately).
</Callout>

**Why:** For any $x \in G$, the element $x$ has finite order $n$ (by Lagrange's theorem), so $x^{-1} = x^{n-1}$ — already a product of copies of $x$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Generators of S₃">
The symmetric group $S_3$ has order $6$. It can be generated by just two elements: a $3$-cycle $(1\,2\,3)$ and a transposition $(1\,2)$.
The inverse of $(1\,2\,3)$ is $(1\,3\,2) = (1\,2\,3)^2$, so no explicit inverse needed.
</Callout>

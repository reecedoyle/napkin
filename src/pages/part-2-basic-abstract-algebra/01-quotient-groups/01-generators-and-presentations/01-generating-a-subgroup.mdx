<SlideLayout title="Generating a subgroup" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Generators and group presentations">

We already know that for any element $x$ in a group $G$, we can form the cyclic subgroup

$$
\{ \dots, x^{-2}, x^{-1}, 1, x, x^2, \dots \}
$$

Think of it this way: **put $x$ in a box, seal it tightly, and shake vigorously**. The element $x$ alone, combined with itself and its inverse over and over, generates this subgroup.

What if we put *two* elements $x$ and $y$ in the box? Now we can form any finite product of $x$, $y$, $x^{-1}$, $y^{-1}$:

$$
xyxyx, \quad x^2 y^9 x^{-5} y^3, \quad y^{-2015}, \quad \dots
$$

<Callout kind="definition" title="Subgroup generated by a set">
Let $S$ be a subset of a group $G$. The **subgroup generated by $S$**, denoted <Term k="generatedS">$\langle S \rangle$</Term>, is the set of all elements expressible as a finite product of elements in $S$ and their inverses.

If $\langle S \rangle = G$, we say $S$ is a set of <Term k="generators">**generators**</Term> for $G$.
</Callout>

For example, $\{1\}$ generates $\mathbb{Z} = (\mathbb{Z}, +)$: every integer is a sum of $1$'s and $-1$'s.

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="The textbook approach (and why it obscures)" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · (Digression) The first isomorphism theorem">

Most textbooks take a different route:

1. **Define** normal using $gng^{-1} \in N$ directly (without motivating where this came from).
2. **Define** $G/N$ via coset multiplication — which looks mysterious.
3. **State** the first isomorphism theorem as a result.

This approach makes normal subgroups look like they "come out of thin air". The connection to homomorphisms and kernels — which is the entire motivation — gets buried.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Napkin's take">
The approach in these slides goes the other direction: start with a surjective homomorphism $\phi: G \twoheadrightarrow Q$, observe the kernel $N$, define the quotient to recover $Q$, and then extract the algebraic condition on $N$ after the fact. This makes every step *motivated*.
</Callout>

The theorem that connects the two approaches is the **first isomorphism theorem**.

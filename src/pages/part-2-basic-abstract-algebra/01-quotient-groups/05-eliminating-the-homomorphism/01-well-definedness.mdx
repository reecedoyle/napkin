<SlideLayout title="Well-definedness of coset multiplication" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Eliminating the homomorphism">

We defined $(g_1 N)(g_2 N) = (g_1 g_2)N$. The trouble: cosets don't have unique representatives. If $g_1 N = g_1' N$ and $g_2 N = g_2' N$, we need $(g_1 g_2)N = (g_1' g_2')N$.

With a homomorphism $\phi$ in the background, this is automatic: $\phi(g_i) = \phi(g_i')$ implies $\phi(g_1 g_2) = \phi(g_1)\phi(g_2) = \phi(g_1')\phi(g_2') = \phi(g_1' g_2')$.

But what property of $N$ alone ensures this?

<Callout kind="remark" title="The critical question">
Where do we **actually use** the fact that $N$ is normal (i.e., a kernel)? The coset definition $(g_1 N)(g_2 N) = (g_1 g_2)N$ mentions no $\phi$ at all. The normality of $N$ is the hidden guarantee that multiplication is well-defined.
</Callout>

Suppose $\phi: G \to K$ has kernel $H$. There is an obvious property $H$ inherits:

$$
h \in H,\; g \in G \;\Longrightarrow\; ghg^{-1} \in H.
$$

**Proof:** $\phi(ghg^{-1}) = \phi(g)\phi(h)\phi(g)^{-1} = \phi(g) \cdot 1_K \cdot \phi(g)^{-1} = 1_K$.

This property — <Term k="conjugate">**closure under conjugation**</Term> — turns out to be both necessary and sufficient for $N$ to be a kernel.

</SlideLayout>

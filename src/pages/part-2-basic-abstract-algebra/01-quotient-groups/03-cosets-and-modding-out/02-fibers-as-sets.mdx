<SlideLayout title="Fibers of a homomorphism">

Continuing the mod-100 example, let $N = 100\mathbb{Z}$. Look at the sets of integers that all map to the same residue:

$$
\begin{aligned}
N   &= \{ \dots, -200, -100, 0, 100, 200, \dots \} \\
1+N &= \{ \dots, -199, -99, 1, 101, 201, \dots \} \\
2+N &= \{ \dots, -198, -98, 2, 102, 202, \dots \} \\
    &\;\vdots \\
99+N &= \{ \dots, -101, -1, 99, 199, 299, \dots \}
\end{aligned}
$$

Every element in one row maps to the **same** residue under $\phi$; elements in different rows map to **different** residues.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Key idea">
We can think of $\mathbb{Z}/100\mathbb{Z}$ as the group whose **elements are these 100 sets** (the "fibers" of $\phi$), rather than the numbers $0, 1, \dots, 99$. The group operation on fibers mirrors addition of residues.
</Callout>

This partitioning into fibers is the heart of the quotient construction. The fibers are exactly the **cosets** of $N$, which we define next.

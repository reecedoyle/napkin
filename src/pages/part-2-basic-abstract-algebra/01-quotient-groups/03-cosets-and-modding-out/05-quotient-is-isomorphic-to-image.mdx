<SlideLayout title="The quotient captures the image" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Cosets and modding out">

The canonical map $\pi: G \to G/N$ defined by $\pi(g) = gN$ is a <Term k="canonicalProjection">**surjective homomorphism**</Term> with kernel exactly $N$.

So by construction, $G/N \cong Q$ for any $Q$ that arises as the image of a surjective homomorphism $G \twoheadrightarrow Q$ with kernel $N$. The diagram is:

$$
G \xrightarrow{\;\phi\;} Q \qquad \text{with } \ker\phi = N \quad\Longrightarrow\quad G/N \cong Q.
$$

<Callout kind="example" title="mod 100 revisited">
The map $\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/100\mathbb{Z}$ has kernel $100\mathbb{Z}$. The quotient group $\mathbb{Z}/100\mathbb{Z}$ is literally defined as $\mathbb{Z}/(100\mathbb{Z})$: cosets of $100\mathbb{Z}$ in $\mathbb{Z}$, with coset addition.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Think of G as fibers over G/N">
Imagine $G$ drawn as a collection of equally-tall fibers (one per coset of $N$). The quotient map $\pi$ collapses each fiber to a single point. The resulting base is $G/N$.
</Callout>

In a sense, $G/N$ is "$G$ with the condition $n = 1$ imposed for every $n \in N$".

</SlideLayout>

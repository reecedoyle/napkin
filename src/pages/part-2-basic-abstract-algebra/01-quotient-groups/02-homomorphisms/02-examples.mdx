<SlideLayout title="Examples of homomorphisms" part="Part II — Basic Abstract Algebra" chapter="3. Quotient groups · Homomorphisms">

<Callout kind="example" title="A catalogue of homomorphisms">
Let $G$ and $H$ be groups.

- **Identity / isomorphism:** any isomorphism $G \to H$ is a homomorphism; so is $\mathrm{id}_G: G \to G$.
- **Trivial homomorphism:** $G \to H$ by $g \mapsto 1_H$ — sends everything to the identity.
- **Mod 100:** $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/100\mathbb{Z}$ by $n \mapsto \overline{n}$ — each integer to its residue mod 100.
- **Scaling:** $\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$ by $x \mapsto 10x$ — injective but not surjective.
- **Embedding:** $S_n \to S_{n+1}$ treating every permutation on $\{1,\dots,n\}$ as one that fixes $n+1$.
- **Dihedral reduction:** $\phi: D_{12} \to D_6$ by $s_{12} \mapsto s_6$, $r_{12} \mapsto r_6$.
</Callout>

The dihedral and generator examples illustrate a key point:

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Homomorphisms from presented groups">
To specify a homomorphism $\phi: G \to H$ when $G = \langle x_1, \dots, x_k \mid \text{relations} \rangle$, it suffices to:
1. Say where each generator $x_i$ goes.
2. Check every relation still holds in $H$ at those images.
</Callout>

For instance, specifying a homomorphism $\mathbb{Z} \to G$ is the same as specifying the image of $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$, since $\phi(n) = \phi(1)^n$ is forced.

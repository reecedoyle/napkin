Given two <Term k="metricSpace">metric spaces</Term> $M = (M, d_M)$ and $N = (N, d_N)$, can we make their Cartesian product $M \times N$ into a metric space? The prototype is $\RR \times \RR$, which we already know carries the <Term k="euclideanMetric">Euclidean metric</Term> as $\RR^2$ — so the question is really how to do this in general, and which formula to pick.

For points $p_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $p_2 = (x_2, y_2)$ in $M \times N$, here are three natural candidates:

$$
\begin{align*}
d_{\text{max}}(p_1, p_2) &\defeq \max\bigl\{ d_M(x_1, x_2),\ d_N(y_1, y_2) \bigr\} \\
d_{\text{Euclid}}(p_1, p_2) &\defeq \sqrt{d_M(x_1, x_2)^2 + d_N(y_1, y_2)^2} \\
d_{\text{taxicab}}(p_1, p_2) &\defeq d_M(x_1, x_2) + d_N(y_1, y_2).
\end{align*}
$$

All three obey the metric axioms. When $M = N = \RR$, the middle one is the familiar Euclidean distance on the plane; the bottom is the <Term k="taxicabMetric">taxicab metric</Term>; and the top is the "max"-distance — the larger of the two coordinate gaps.

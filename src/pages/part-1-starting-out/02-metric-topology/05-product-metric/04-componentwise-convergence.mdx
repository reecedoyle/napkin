<SlideLayout title="Convergence is componentwise" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="2. Metric Topology · Product metric">

The product metric plays well with <Term k="convergence">convergence</Term>: a sequence of pairs converges precisely when each coordinate sequence converges.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Componentwise convergence">
A sequence $\bigl( (x_n, y_n) \bigr)$ in $M \times N$ converges to $(x, y)$ if and only if $x_n \to x$ in $M$ and $y_n \to y$ in $N$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Using the $d_{\text{max}}$ form of the product metric,
$$
d_{\text{max}}\bigl( (x, y),\ (x_n, y_n) \bigr) = \max\bigl\{ d_M(x, x_n),\ d_N(y, y_n) \bigr\}.
$$
The left-hand side tends to $0$ as $n \to \infty$ if and only if **both** of $d_M(x, x_n)$ and $d_N(y, y_n)$ tend to $0$ — which is exactly the statement that $x_n \to x$ in $M$ and $y_n \to y$ in $N$. $\blacksquare$

The proof is one line because the max of two non-negative quantities shrinks to $0$ iff each of them does. Choosing $d_{\text{max}}$ as our representative of the product metric makes this transparent; the same is true for $d_{\text{Euclid}}$ or $d_{\text{taxicab}}$ (they are sandwiched between $d_{\text{max}}$ and $2\,d_{\text{max}}$, so shrink to $0$ at the same time), but the bookkeeping is uglier.

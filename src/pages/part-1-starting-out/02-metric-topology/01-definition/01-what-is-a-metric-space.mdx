<SlideLayout title="What is a metric space?">

Metric topology is the morally correct way to motivate point-set topology — and to generalize ordinary calculus. The starting object is very concrete: a set of points equipped with a notion of *distance* between them.

A <Term k="metricSpace">metric space $(M, d)$</Term> packages two pieces of data:

- a set of points $M$, and
- a <Term k="metric">distance function $d$</Term> that takes two points and returns a non-negative real number.

The distance function has to behave the way distance ought to behave — symmetric, never negative, zero only between a point and itself, and never longer to go direct than to detour.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Prototypical example">
Keep in mind the Euclidean plane <Term k="RRn">$\RR^2$</Term>: points are pairs $(x, y)$, and $d$ is straight-line distance. Everything in this section is built around generalisations and variations of this picture.
</Callout>

Throughout this chapter, draw lots of pictures. Metric spaces are very visual objects.

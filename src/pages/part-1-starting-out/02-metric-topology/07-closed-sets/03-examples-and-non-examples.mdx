Let's stock up on (non-)examples to anchor the definition.

<Callout kind="example" title="Examples of closed sets">
- The empty set $\varnothing$ is closed in $M$ for vacuous reasons: there are no sequences whose terms lie in $\varnothing$, so there is nothing to check.
- The whole space $M$ is closed in $M$ for tautological reasons: any limit $x \in M$ is automatically in $M$.
- The closed interval $[0, 1]$ is <Term k="closedSet">closed</Term> in $\RR$ — hence the name! This is the prototype to keep in mind. Any sequence in $[0,1]$ that converges in $\RR$ must have its limit in $[0,1]$, by the usual order properties of the reals.
- In fact, $[0, 1]$ (viewed as $[0,1] \times \{0\} \subseteq \RR^2$) is even closed in $\RR^2$.
</Callout>

The first non-example is the classic one — the open interval misses its endpoints.

<Callout kind="example" title="Non-example: open intervals">
Let $S = (0, 1)$ denote the open interval. Then $S$ is **not** closed in $\RR$, because the sequence
$$
\tfrac{1}{2}, \; \tfrac{1}{4}, \; \tfrac{1}{8}, \; \dots
$$
lies entirely in $S$ but converges to $0 \in \RR$, and $0 \notin (0, 1)$. A sequence in $S$ escaped to a point outside $S$.
</Callout>

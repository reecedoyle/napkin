<SlideLayout title="Neither open nor closed (and both)">

A word of warning about this terminology, since the names are deceptive.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Closed is NOT the same as not-open">
**Most subsets of a metric space are neither open nor closed.** "Closed" is not the logical negation of "open" — they are two different conditions, and a generic set will fail both.
</Callout>

The cleanest illustration is the half-open interval:

<Callout kind="example" title="A set that is neither open nor closed">
The half-open interval $[0, 1)$ is neither <Term k="openSet">open</Term> nor <Term k="closedSet">closed</Term> in $\RR$.

- It is **not open**: any $r$-neighborhood $(-r, r)$ of the endpoint $0$ contains negative numbers, so no neighborhood of $0$ fits inside $[0, 1)$.
- It is **not closed**: the sequence $1 - \tfrac{1}{n}$ lies entirely in $[0, 1)$ but converges to $1 \notin [0, 1)$.

Both defects show up at different endpoints, and each on its own would already disqualify the set.
</Callout>

At the other extreme, it is also possible for a set to be **both** open and closed. The empty set $\varnothing$ and the whole space $M$ are always both — they satisfy the open and closed conditions vacuously and tautologically. Whether there are *other* such sets is a surprisingly deep question; it is connected to the topological notion of *connectedness*, which we'll meet in a later chapter on topology.

So the four possible (open?, closed?) combinations all really occur:

| Subset of $\RR$ | Open? | Closed? |
| :-- | :--: | :--: |
| $(0, 1)$ | yes | no |
| $[0, 1]$ | no | yes |
| $[0, 1)$ | no | no |
| $\varnothing$, $\RR$ | yes | yes |

Once you internalise this you'll stop expecting these adjectives to be opposites, and you'll be ready for the theorem on the next slide — which explains where the *names* "open" and "closed" come from in the first place.

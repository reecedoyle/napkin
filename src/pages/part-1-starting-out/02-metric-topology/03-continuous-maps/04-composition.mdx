Here is an example of how much easier sequential continuity makes life. The fact that a composition of continuous functions is continuous would be a bit of an $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ slog from the original definition; with sequences it is essentially a one-liner.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Composition of continuous maps">
Let $f \colon M \to N$ and $g \colon N \to L$ be <Term k="continuous">continuous</Term> maps of metric spaces. Then the composition $g \circ f \colon M \to L$ is continuous.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Let $p \in M$ be arbitrary, and let $(x_n)$ be any sequence in $M$ with $x_n \to p$. By Sequential continuity above applied to $f$, we have $f(x_n) \to f(p)$ in $N$. Applying it again — this time to $g$ at the point $f(p)$ — we get $g(f(x_n)) \to g(f(p))$ in $L$. That is, $(g \circ f)(x_n) \to (g \circ f)(p)$ for every convergent sequence $x_n \to p$. By Sequential continuity once more (this time in the reverse direction), $g \circ f$ is continuous at $p$. Since $p$ was arbitrary, $g \circ f$ is continuous. $\blacksquare$

The technique is worth absorbing: a question of the form "is this composite map continuous?" reduces to chaining convergence statements, with no $\varepsilon$'s or $\delta$'s in sight. This is the single biggest payoff of having an equivalent definition.

All we have to do is replace the absolute values from the calculus definition with the more general distance functions. This gives us a definition of continuity for *any* function between metric spaces.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Continuous map">
Let $M = (M, d_M)$ and $N = (N, d_N)$ be metric spaces. A function $f \colon M \to N$ is **<Term k="continuous">continuous</Term>** at a point $p \in M$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that
$$
d_M(x, p) < \delta \;\implies\; d_N(f(x), f(p)) < \varepsilon.
$$

The function $f$ is **continuous** (without qualification) if it is continuous at every point $p \in M$.
</Callout>

Notice the asymmetry: $d_M$ measures distance between *inputs* (which live in $M$), and $d_N$ measures distance between *outputs* (which live in $N$). This is reminiscent of how the definition of a group isomorphism uses one operation on the source and a different one on the target — each side gets the structure native to its own space.

The generalisation is useful because it tells us immediately how to carry continuity arguments from $\RR$ over to more general spaces like <Term k="CC">$\CC$</Term> or any of the metric spaces we have already met.

In calculus you almost certainly met — or at least heard of — the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition of a continuous function. It looked something like this:

<Callout kind="remark" title="From calculus">
A function $f \colon \RR \to \RR$ is **<Term k="continuous">continuous</Term>** at a point $p \in \RR$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that
$$
|x - p| < \delta \;\implies\; |f(x) - f(p)| < \varepsilon.
$$
</Callout>

Read this slowly. The two absolute values play *different* roles: the first measures distance between inputs (in the domain), and the second measures distance between outputs (in the codomain). On $\RR$, both happen to be computed by the same formula $|x - y|$, but conceptually they are separate.

That observation is the key to generalising. We have already developed a notion of distance in an arbitrary <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term> — so any time we see "$|x - y|$" in a calculus definition, we should ask whether it can be replaced by a metric. Try the guess yourself.

The crucial point is that the two distance functions live in different spaces — d_M operates on inputs, d_N on outputs — so there is no ambiguity about which metric appears where."

<SlideLayout title="Sequential continuity" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="2. Metric Topology · Continuous maps">

The $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition is correct, but cumbersome — it makes heavy use of the real numbers (those $\varepsilon$'s and $\delta$'s) even when the spaces $M$ and $N$ have nothing to do with $\RR$. Happily, there is an equivalent characterisation purely in terms of <Term k="convergence">convergence</Term> of sequences, which is often much easier to apply.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Sequential continuity">
A function $f \colon M \to N$ of metric spaces is continuous at a point $p \in M$ if and only if the following holds:

For every sequence $x_1, x_2, \dots$ in $M$ with $x_n \to p$, the sequence $f(x_1), f(x_2), \dots$ in $N$ satisfies $f(x_n) \to f(p)$.
</Callout>

In words: $f$ is continuous at $p$ iff $f$ preserves convergence to $p$.

**Proof.** There are two directions. The forward direction — assuming $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ continuity and deducing the sequential statement — is a short unpacking of definitions, and is left as an exercise.

We want to show f(x_n) -> f(p) in N, i.e. for every epsilon > 0 there exists N such that d_N(f(x_n), f(p)) < epsilon for every n >= N. Fix epsilon > 0. By epsilon-delta continuity of f at p, there exists delta > 0 such that d_M(x, p) < delta implies d_N(f(x), f(p)) < epsilon. Since x_n -> p in M, applied to *this* delta we get some integer N with d_M(x_n, p) < delta for all n >= N. For each such n, the implication gives d_N(f(x_n), f(p)) < epsilon. That is exactly the required N for the convergence of f(x_n) to f(p). Done."

For the converse, we prove the contrapositive: if $f$ is **not** $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ continuous at $p$, then it fails to preserve convergence to $p$.

Suppose $f$ is not continuous at $p$. Negating the definition, there is some "bad" $\varepsilon > 0$ — fix it — for which no $\delta > 0$ works. In particular, for each $\delta = 1/n$ (with $n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$) there is some point $x_n \in M$ with
$$
d_M(x_n, p) < \tfrac{1}{n}, \quad \text{yet} \quad d_N(f(x_n), f(p)) \ge \varepsilon.
$$
The first inequality says $x_n \to p$ in $M$ (the distances shrink below any prescribed bound). The second says $f(x_n)$ stays at least $\varepsilon$ away from $f(p)$ — for every $n$ — so $f(x_n) \not\to f(p)$. This is exactly a failure of the sequential condition. $\blacksquare$

<SlideLayout title="Is 1/x continuous? An Internet debate">

A discursive problem to close the chapter. A few years back, an innocent-looking question on the Internet — "is $1/x$ a continuous function?" — generated a remarkable amount of online controversy. The interesting thing isn't the answer; it's *why* the question divides people. The whole disagreement comes from one thing: the question is ill-formed, and different readers patch it up differently before answering.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The general lesson">
A function is *not* a formula. A function is a triple: a domain, a codomain, and a rule. Most Internet "math debates" dissolve as soon as you make people commit to the domain. "Continuous" is an adjective for functions; if you haven't specified the domain, you don't yet have a function, and the question doesn't have an answer.
</Callout>

  prompt="Someone on the Internet posted the question 'is 1/x a continuous function?', leading to great controversy on Twitter. How should you respond?"
  hint="First answer the prior question: 'is 1/x a function?'."
  solution={`Like most Internet 'debates' about math, the disagreement comes from sloppy definitions. The original (ill-formed) question is:

(1) Is 1/x a continuous function?

To make it well-formed, you should first ask:

(2) Is 1/x a function?

This question is also ill-formed, because the specification of a function includes its domain. One reasonable guess at what the asker meant is to take the domain to be R \\ {0} (the nonzero reals), giving:

(2') Does 1/x define a function from R \\ {0} to R?

The answer here is a firm YES.

On the other hand, 1/x does NOT make sense as a function on all of R. Specifying a function requires an output for every input — and at least if you want a real-valued function, there's no way to assign an output to the input 0.

Now, returning to (1), we can ask the well-formed version:

(1') Does 1/x describe a continuous function from R \\ {0} to R?

Again a firm YES.

You could also ask 'does 1/x describe a continuous function R → R?'. But this is misleading: it's like asking 'is sqrt(2) an even integer?'. The question doesn't make sense to begin with, because sqrt(2) isn't an integer, and 'even' is an adjective used for integers — so trying to apply 'even' to sqrt(2) is a type-error. Similarly, 'continuous' is an adjective used for functions; it doesn't make sense to ask whether it applies to something that isn't a function.

(See the links in the callout above for the original Twitter post and Reddit thread.)`}

<SlideLayout title="The open-set condition for continuity">

The whole upshot of this section is the following beautiful characterisation of continuity. It dispenses with $\varepsilon$ and $\delta$ entirely, replacing them with the single notion of an open set.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Open-set condition for continuity">
A function $f \colon M \to N$ of <Term k="metricSpace">metric spaces</Term> is <Term k="continuous">continuous</Term> if and only if the pre-image of every <Term k="openSet">open set</Term> in $N$ is open in $M$. That is, for every open $V \subseteq N$,
$$
f^{-1}(V) \mathrel{:=} \{ x \in M : f(x) \in V \}
$$
is open in $M$.
</Callout>

A picture sums up the situation. Pick any point $x$ in the pre-image $U = f^{-1}(V)$, with image $y = f(x) \in V$. Openness of $V$ gives a small $\varepsilon$-ball around $y$ inside $V$; continuity of $f$ at $x$ then gives a $\delta$-ball around $x$ that $f$ pushes into that $\varepsilon$-ball — and hence into $V$ — and hence into $U$.

So the open-set condition packages the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition *globally*: rather than checking continuity point by point, you check that pre-images of open sets are open — a single statement about *sets* rather than a quantifier soup about *points*.

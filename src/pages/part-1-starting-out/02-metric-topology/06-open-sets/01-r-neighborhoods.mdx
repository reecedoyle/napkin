<Term k="continuous">Continuity</Term> is really about what happens *locally*: how a function behaves "close to a point $p$". To make "close" precise we have already been using the metric — and from the metric we get a natural notion of a small ball of radius $r$ around any point.

<Callout kind="definition" title="r-neighborhood">
Let $(M, d)$ be a <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term>. For each real number $r > 0$ and point $p \in M$, define
$$
M_r(p) \mathrel{:=} \{ x \in M : d(x, p) < r \}.
$$
The set $M_r(p)$ is called the <Term k="rNeighborhood">$r$-neighborhood</Term> of $p$. (It is also called the *open ball of radius $r$ centred at $p$*.)
</Callout>

The strict inequality $d(x, p) < r$ is essential — the boundary points at distance *exactly* $r$ are excluded.

In $\RR$ with the usual metric, $\RR_r(p)$ is exactly the open interval $(p - r, p + r)$. In $\RR^2$ with the Euclidean metric, $(\RR^2)_r(p)$ is the open disk of radius $r$ centred at $p$. The notation generalises both.

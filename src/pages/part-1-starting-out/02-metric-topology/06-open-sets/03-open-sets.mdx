<SlideLayout title="Open sets">

The rephrased continuity from the previous slide motivates the central definition of this section. The pre-image of an $\varepsilon$-neighborhood always has the property that *some* $\delta$-neighborhood fits inside it around every point — sets with this property deserve a name.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Open set">
A subset $U \subseteq M$ is <Term k="openSet">open</Term> in $M$ if for each $p \in U$ there exists $r > 0$ such that
$$
M_r(p) \subseteq U.
$$
In words: every point of $U$ has at least one <Term k="rNeighborhood">$r$-neighborhood</Term> entirely contained in $U$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Notation">
A set being open is defined *relative to* the parent metric space $M$ — the same set of points can be open in one ambient space and not in another. When the parent space is clear from context we abbreviate "open in $M$" to just "open". When it matters, we say it explicitly.
</Callout>

The prototypical example is the open disk in the plane:

Every point $p$ inside the dashed unit circle has *some* dotted neighborhood that still fits inside — perhaps tiny if $p$ is near the boundary, but a positive radius always exists. The boundary circle itself is *not* part of the set; that is what makes the inequality strict, and what makes the set open.

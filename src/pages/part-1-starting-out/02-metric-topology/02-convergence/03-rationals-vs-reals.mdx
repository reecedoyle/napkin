<SlideLayout title="Convergence depends on the parent space">

The remark on the previous slide warned that the limit has to live inside the parent space. Here is the canonical illustration: the same sequence of points can converge in one space and fail to converge in another, depending on whether the limit is "there to receive it".

<Callout kind="example" title="Decimal approximations to √2">
Consider the sequence
$$
x_1 = 1, \quad x_2 = 1.4, \quad x_3 = 1.41, \quad x_4 = 1.414, \quad \dots
$$
of successive decimal truncations of $\sqrt{2}$.

- **(a)** Viewed as a sequence in <Term k="RR">$\RR$</Term>, it converges — its limit is $\sqrt{2} \in \RR$.
- **(b)** However, every $x_i$ is rational, so we could just as well view the sequence as living in <Term k="QQ">$\QQ$</Term>. As a sequence in $\QQ$, it does **NOT** converge!
</Callout>

The reason for (b) is precisely the parenthetical caveat from the previous slide. In $\QQ$, with the metric inherited from $\RR$, the "natural" target $\sqrt{2}$ is simply not a point of the space. And one can check that no other rational $q$ works either: pick $q \ne \sqrt{2}$, and once $\varepsilon$ is smaller than $|q - \sqrt{2}|$, the tail of the sequence leaves the $\varepsilon$-ball around $q$ forever. The sequence has nowhere in $\QQ$ to converge to.

This shows that convergence is genuinely a property of the *pair* (sequence, parent space), not of the sequence alone. Metric spaces that have "enough" limits — every sequence whose terms get arbitrarily close to each other actually converges — are called **complete**; we will meet them properly later.

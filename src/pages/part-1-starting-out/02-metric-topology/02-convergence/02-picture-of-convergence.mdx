It helps to draw a picture. Below are nine points $x_1, x_2, \dots, x_9$ of a sequence converging to $x$. The dashed circle is an <Term k="rNeighborhood">$r$-neighborhood</Term> of $x$ — the set of points within distance $r$. As $n$ grows, the terms eventually enter (and stay inside) the circle, no matter how small $r$ is. That is exactly the definition: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, all but finitely many terms lie inside the $\varepsilon$-neighborhood of $x$.

Once $n$ is large enough — past some threshold $N$ that depends on $r$ — every $x_n$ sits inside the dashed circle. Shrink $r$, and you just have to wait a bit longer; the existence of *some* such $N$ for *every* $r$ is what makes the sequence converge.

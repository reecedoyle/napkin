<SlideLayout title="Convergence in a metric space">

Now that we have a notion of distance between two points, we can talk about what it means for a sequence of points to *converge*. The story is the same as the familiar epsilon-$N$ definition on the real line, except the absolute value $|x_n - x|$ is replaced by the distance function $d(x_n, x)$.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Convergence">
Let $(x_n)_{n \ge 1}$ be a sequence of points in a <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term> $M$. We say that $x_n$ **<Term k="convergence">converges</Term>** to $x$ if the following holds:

For every $\varepsilon > 0$, there is an integer $N$ (depending on $\varepsilon$) such that
$$
d(x_n, x) < \varepsilon \quad \text{for every } n \ge N.
$$

We write this as
$$
x_n \to x \qquad \text{or equivalently} \qquad \lim_{n \to \infty} x_n = x.
$$

A sequence **converges in $M$** if it converges to some point $x \in M$.
</Callout>

You should pause and check that this definition matches your intuition for sequences on the real line: "eventually, the terms are all within $\varepsilon$ of the target, no matter how small you pick $\varepsilon$."

<Callout kind="remark" title="Notation">
If the parent space $M$ is understood, we will allow ourselves to abbreviate "converges in $M$" to just "converges". Keep in mind, though, that convergence is defined *relative to* the parent space — the limit point must actually live in $M$ for the sequence to converge there. We will see in a moment that this caveat has real bite.
</Callout>

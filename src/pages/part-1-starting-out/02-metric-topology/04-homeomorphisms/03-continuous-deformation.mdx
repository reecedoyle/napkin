<SlideLayout title="Continuous deformation — circles and squares" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="2. Metric Topology · Homeomorphisms">

The topologist's intuition for homeomorphism is **continuous deformation**: two spaces are homeomorphic when you can bend, stretch, and squish one onto the other without tearing or gluing.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The doughnut and the coffee cup">
The most famous example is that a doughnut (a solid torus) is homeomorphic to a coffee cup with one handle. Imagine slowly squishing the doughnut: most of it collapses into the cup's body, and the hole in the middle deforms continuously into the hole in the handle. No tearing, no gluing — the punchline that "a topologist cannot tell a coffee cup from a doughnut" really is a theorem in this sense.
</Callout>

A more workaday example: the unit circle is homeomorphic to the boundary of a square. Topologically, "round" and "with corners" don't matter — the four corners can be smoothed out by a continuous map and put back by a continuous inverse.

<Callout kind="example" title="A bijection between the circle and a square boundary">
Inscribe the unit circle $S^1$ inside a square of "radius" $1.4$ (centred at the origin, sides at $x = \pm 1.4$, $y = \pm 1.4$). For each point $P$ on $S^1$, shoot a ray from the origin through $P$; it hits the square's boundary at a unique point $Q$. The map $P \mapsto Q$ is a continuous bijection from the circle to the square's boundary, and the inverse — "shoot back along the ray from the origin until you hit the circle" — is also continuous. So $S^1$ is homeomorphic to $\partial([-1.4, 1.4]^2)$.
</Callout>

The identity map is the most boring homeomorphism of all — any space $M$ is homeomorphic to itself via $\mathrm{id} \colon M \to M$. That makes "is homeomorphic to" reflexive, as promised.

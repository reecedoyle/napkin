<SlideLayout title="When two metric spaces are 'the same'" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="2. Metric Topology · Homeomorphisms">

When do we consider two groups to be the same? Answer: there is a structure-preserving map between them which is also a bijection — an <Term k="isomorphic">isomorphism</Term>. For metric spaces we do exactly the same thing, but replace "structure-preserving" with "<Term k="continuous">continuous</Term>".

<Callout kind="definition" title="Homeomorphism">
Let $M$ and $N$ be metric spaces. A function $f \colon M \to N$ is a **<Term k="homeomorphism">homeomorphism</Term>** if it is a bijection, and **both** $f \colon M \to N$ and its inverse $f^{-1} \colon N \to M$ are continuous.

When such an $f$ exists, we say $M$ and $N$ are **homeomorphic**.
</Callout>

The slogan: a homeomorphism is a "continuous renaming" of the points of one space as the points of another. The two spaces may look very different as sets — different points, different metrics, perhaps different ambient dimensions — but homeomorphism says they have the same *topological* structure.

Three checks are bundled into the definition:

- $f$ is a bijection (so it really is a renaming of points),
- $f$ is continuous (no "tearing" when we go $M \to N$),
- $f^{-1}$ is continuous (no "tearing" when we go back $N \to M$).

It is a quick check that "is homeomorphic to" is an equivalence relation on metric spaces — reflexive via the identity, symmetric by swapping $f$ and $f^{-1}$, and transitive because composing two homeomorphisms gives a third (use that compositions of continuous maps are continuous, on both $f$ and the inverses).

</SlideLayout>

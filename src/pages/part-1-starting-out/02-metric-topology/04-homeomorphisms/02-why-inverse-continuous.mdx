<SlideLayout title="Why the inverse must also be continuous">

You might be surprised that the definition demands $f^{-1}$ be continuous on top of $f$ being a continuous bijection. Why isn't continuity in one direction enough?

The cleanest way to see it is by comparison with group isomorphisms. There, once you have a bijective homomorphism $\phi \colon G \to H$, a short calculation shows $\phi^{-1}$ automatically respects the group operation — so the inverse is "free". For continuous bijections this freebie does **not** hold: there are genuinely continuous bijections whose set-theoretic inverse fails to be continuous.

<Callout kind="example" title="Homeomorphism ≠ continuous bijection">
Two warning examples:

- **A line wrapping onto a circle.** There is a continuous bijection $[0, 1) \to S^1$ sending $t$ to the point on the unit circle at angle $2\pi t$. As $t$ approaches $1$ from below, its image approaches the image of $0$ — so the inverse map would have to send those nearby circle points to wildly distant values of $t$ (near $1$ vs. near $0$). The inverse is **not** continuous, so this is not a homeomorphism.
- **A discrete space the size of $\RR$.** Let $M$ be a <Term k="discreteMetric">discrete metric space</Term> with $|M| = |\RR|$. Pick any bijection $f \colon M \to \RR$. From the previous section, *every* map out of a discrete space is continuous, so $f$ is a continuous bijection. But the inverse $\RR \to M$ is certainly not continuous: in $\RR$ points can sit arbitrarily close together, while in $M$ distinct points are forced to be exactly $1$ apart.
</Callout>

So the two-sided continuity in the definition of a homeomorphism is doing real work — without it, "$f$ is a continuous bijection" would lump together spaces that have genuinely different topological behaviour.

The axioms only say that *an* identity exists, and that *an* inverse exists for each element. They don't immediately say these are unique. As it turns out, they are.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Uniqueness of identity and inverses">
Let $G$ be a group.

1. The identity of $G$ is unique.
2. The inverse of any element $g \in G$ is unique.
3. For any $g \in G$, $(g^{-1})^{-1} = g$.
</Callout>

This is mostly formal manipulation; you needn't feel bad skipping it on a first read.

**Proof.**

*(a)* If $1$ and $1'$ are both identities, then
$$ 1 = 1 \star 1' = 1', $$
since $1'$ acts as identity on the left of the first equation and $1$ acts as identity on the right of the second.

*(b)* Suppose $h$ and $h'$ are both inverses of $g$. Then $g \star h = 1_G$, so
$$ h' = h' \star 1_G = h' \star (g \star h) = (h' \star g) \star h = 1_G \star h = h. $$

*(c)* The element $g$ satisfies $g^{-1} \star g = g \star g^{-1} = 1_G$, which is exactly the equation that says $g$ is the inverse of $g^{-1}$. By uniqueness, $(g^{-1})^{-1} = g$.

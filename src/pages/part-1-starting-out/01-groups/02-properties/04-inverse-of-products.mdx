Now a slightly more useful fact. To invert a product, you invert each factor — but the order *flips*.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Inverse of a product">
Let $G$ be a group, and let $a, b \in G$. Then
$$ (ab)^{-1} = b^{-1} a^{-1}. $$
</Callout>

The flipped order is what you'd expect if you think of $a$ and $b$ as operations: to undo "do $a$, then do $b$", you must first undo $b$, then undo $a$. Putting on socks then shoes is reversed by removing shoes then socks.

  prompt="Prove that (ab)^{-1} = b^{-1} a^{-1} in any group G."
  solution="By the uniqueness of inverses (previous slide), it's enough to check that b^{-1} a^{-1} multiplied by ab gives the identity, on both sides.

Right multiplication: (ab)(b^{-1} a^{-1}) = a (b b^{-1}) a^{-1} = a · 1_G · a^{-1} = a a^{-1} = 1_G. (We used associativity to regroup, then b b^{-1} = 1_G, then the identity axiom, then a a^{-1} = 1_G.)

Left multiplication: similarly, (b^{-1} a^{-1})(ab) = b^{-1} (a^{-1} a) b = b^{-1} · 1_G · b = b^{-1} b = 1_G.

Since b^{-1} a^{-1} satisfies both inverse equations for ab, and inverses are unique, we conclude (ab)^{-1} = b^{-1} a^{-1}."

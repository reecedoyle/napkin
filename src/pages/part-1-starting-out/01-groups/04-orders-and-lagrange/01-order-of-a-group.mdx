<SlideLayout title="The order of a group">

As is typical in math, we use the word *order* for way too many things. In groups there are **two** distinct notions of order. The first is the size of the whole group.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Order of a group">
The **<Term k="groupOrder">order of a group</Term>** $G$ is the number of elements of $G$, denoted $\left|G\right|$. The order need not be finite, as in $\ZZ$. We say $G$ is a **finite group** to mean exactly that $\left|G\right|$ is finite.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Orders of some familiar groups">
- For a prime $p$, $\left|\Zm{p}\right| = p - 1$. The nonzero residues mod $p$ are $1, 2, \dots, p-1$, and that's $p-1$ of them.
- The symmetric group $S_n$ has order $n!$ (one element per permutation of $n$ symbols).
- The dihedral group $D_{2n}$ acting on a regular $n$-gon has order $2n$ — that's $n$ rotations together with $n$ reflections.
- $\ZZ$ is infinite, so $\left|\ZZ\right| = \infty$.
</Callout>

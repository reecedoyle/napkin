<SlideLayout title="Lagrange's theorem for orders">

What's the last property of $\Zm p$ you remember from olympiad math? **Fermat's little theorem**: for any $a \in \Zm p$,

$$
a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p.
$$

This is no coincidence. *Exactly* the same thing happens in every finite group, with $|G|$ playing the role of $p - 1$.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Lagrange's theorem for orders">
Let $G$ be any finite group. Then for every $x \in G$,

$$
x^{\left|G\right|} = 1_G.
$$
</Callout>

Combined with the previous slide's divisibility fact, this says something remarkable: $\ord x$ must **divide** $|G|$ for *every* $x \in G$. The size of the group constrains the order of every element it contains.

We won't prove this right now — keep the result in mind, and we'll prove it later in this chapter, in its full generality (the more general Lagrange's theorem says every subgroup of a finite group has order dividing $|G|$; the version above is the special case applied to the subgroup generated by $x$).

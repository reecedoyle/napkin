<SlideLayout title="Examples of subgroups" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Subgroups">

Subgroups are everywhere once you start looking. Here are three to keep in mind.

<Callout kind="example" title="Examples of subgroups">
- $2\ZZ$ (the even integers) is a subgroup of $\ZZ$. In fact, the doubling map $n \mapsto 2n$ shows it is *isomorphic* to $\ZZ$ itself!
- Consider again $S_n$, the symmetric group on $n$ elements. Let $T$ be the set of permutations $\tau \colon \{1, \dots, n\} \to \{1, \dots, n\}$ for which $\tau(n) = n$. Then $T$ is a subgroup of $S_n$; in fact, it is isomorphic to $S_{n-1}$ (the permutations of the first $n - 1$ symbols).
- Consider the product group $G \times H$ and the elements
$$
\left\{ (g, 1_H) \mid g \in G \right\}.
$$
This is a subgroup of $G \times H$ (why?). In fact, it is isomorphic to $G$ via the isomorphism $(g, 1_H) \mapsto g$.
</Callout>

There are also two subgroups every group automatically has.

<Callout kind="example" title="Stupid examples of subgroups">
For any group $G$, the trivial group $\{1_G\}$ and the entire group $G$ are subgroups of $G$.
</Callout>

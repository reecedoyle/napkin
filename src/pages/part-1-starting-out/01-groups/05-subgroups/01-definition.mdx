<SlideLayout title="Defining a subgroup" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Subgroups">

Earlier we saw that $\GL_n(\RR)$, the $n \times n$ matrices with nonzero determinant, formed a group under matrix multiplication. But we also saw that a subset of $\GL_n(\RR)$, namely $\SL_n(\RR)$, also formed a group under the same operation. For that reason we say that $\SL_n(\RR)$ is a *subgroup* of $\GL_n(\RR)$.

The definition generalises in exactly the way you would expect.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Subgroup">
Let $G = (G, \star)$ be a group. A **subgroup** of $G$ is exactly what you would expect it to be: a group $H = (H, \star)$ where $H$ is a subset of $G$. It is a **proper subgroup** if $H \neq G$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark">
To specify a group $G$, we needed to tell you both what the set $G$ was and what the operation $\star$ was. But to specify a subgroup $H$ of a given group $G$, we only need to tell you who its elements are: the operation of $H$ is just inherited from the operation of $G$.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

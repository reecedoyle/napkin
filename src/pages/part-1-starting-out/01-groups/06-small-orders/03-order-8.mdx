<SlideLayout title="Groups of order 8" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Small orders">

Order $8$ is the first place the menagerie really opens up. There are **five** groups of order $8$ up to isomorphism — three abelian, two non-abelian.

<Callout kind="example" title="The five groups of order 8">
- $\Zc 8$, the cyclic group on eight elements.
- $\Zc 4 \times \Zc 2$.
- $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 2 \times \Zc 2$.
- $D_8$, the dihedral group of order $8$ (symmetries of a square). Non-abelian.
- $Q_8$, the *quaternion group*. Non-abelian.
</Callout>

The first three are all abelian and pairwise non-isomorphic — they are distinguished, again, by the orders of their elements. ($\Zc 8$ has an element of order $8$; $\Zc 4 \times \Zc 2$ has elements of order $4$ but none of order $8$; in $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 2 \times \Zc 2$ everything non-trivial has order $2$.)

The last entry deserves its own callout.

<Callout kind="example" title="Quaternion group">
The **quaternion group** $Q_8$ consists of the eight elements

$$
\pm 1, \quad \pm i, \quad \pm j, \quad \pm k
$$

subject to the multiplication relations

$$
i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.
$$

These few rules determine the entire multiplication table. For instance, multiplying $ijk = -1$ on the right by $k\inv = -k$ gives $ij = k$, and similar manipulations recover $jk = i$, $ki = j$, together with $ji = -k$, $kj = -i$, $ik = -j$. So $Q_8$ is non-abelian.
</Callout>

Even though $|Q_8| = |D_8| = 8$ and both are non-abelian, $Q_8$ and $D_8$ are *not* isomorphic. In $D_8$ there are several elements of order $2$ (every reflection is its own inverse), while in $Q_8$ the only element of order $2$ is $-1$.

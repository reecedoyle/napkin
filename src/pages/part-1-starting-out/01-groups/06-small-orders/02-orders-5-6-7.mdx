<SlideLayout title="Groups of order 5, 6, and 7" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Small orders">

The cyclic groups continue to dominate, but order $6$ finally breaks the pattern: it is the smallest order that admits a **non-abelian** group.

<Callout kind="example" title="Orders 5 and 7">
- The only group of order $5$ is $\Zc 5$.
- The only group of order $7$ is $\Zc 7$.
</Callout>

Both $5$ and $7$ are prime, and *every group of prime order is cyclic* — a fact you'll prove cleanly with Lagrange's theorem. So there is no room for surprises at orders $5$ or $7$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Order 6">
The two groups of order $6$ are:
- $\Zc 6$, the cyclic group on six elements.
- $S_3$, the symmetric group on three letters. **This is the first non-abelian group.**
</Callout>

A few notes on this list.

- *Where is $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 3$?* It's already there: by the **Chinese remainder theorem**, $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 3 \cong \Zc 6$. Whenever you take a product of cyclic groups whose orders are coprime, you get a single cyclic group of the combined order.

- *Where is $D_6$?* Also already there: $D_6 \cong S_3$. A regular triangle has $3$ rotations and $3$ reflections — $6$ symmetries total — and labelling its vertices $1, 2, 3$ identifies each symmetry with a permutation of $\{1, 2, 3\}$.

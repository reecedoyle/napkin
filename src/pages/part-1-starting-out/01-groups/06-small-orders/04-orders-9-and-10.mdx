We close out the survey with orders $9$ and $10$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Order 9">
There are exactly two groups of order $9$:
- $\Zc 9$, the cyclic group on nine elements.
- $\Zc 3 \times \Zc 3$.
</Callout>

The pattern at order $9 = 3^2$ mirrors the pattern at order $4 = 2^2$: a cyclic group, plus a "square of the prime field" group. As at order $4$, the two are distinguished by element orders — $\Zc 9$ has an element of order $9$, while in $\Zc 3 \times \Zc 3$ every non-identity element has order $3$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Order 10">
There are exactly two groups of order $10$:
- $\Zc{10}$, which by the Chinese remainder theorem is the same as $\Zc 5 \times \Zc 2$.
- $D_{10}$, the dihedral group of order $10$ (symmetries of a regular pentagon). Non-abelian.
</Callout>

The Chinese remainder isomorphism $\Zc{10} \cong \Zc 5 \times \Zc 2$ deserves a moment. The element $(1, 1) \in \Zc 5 \times \Zc 2$ has order $\lcm(5, 2) = 10$, so it generates the whole group — and any cyclic group of order $10$ is $\Zc{10}$.

That wraps up the small-order census. By order $12$ things start to get genuinely complicated (there are five groups of order $12$, including $A_4$), but the patterns you've seen here — primes give cyclic, prime squares give two abelian options, $2 \times \text{prime}$ gives cyclic plus dihedral — keep recurring, and you'll meet the tools (Lagrange, Sylow, classification of finite abelian groups) to predict them in later chapters.

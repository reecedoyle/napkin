<SlideLayout title="Groups of order 1 to 4" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Small orders">

Just for fun, here is a list of all groups of order at most ten — *up to isomorphism*, of course. Two groups that look different but obey the same multiplication table count as the same entry. We start at the bottom and work our way up.

<Callout kind="example" title="Orders 1, 2, 3">
- The only group of order $1$ is the **trivial group** $\{1\}$.
- The only group of order $2$ is $\Zc 2$.
- The only group of order $3$ is $\Zc 3$.
</Callout>

For each of these orders the answer is forced. With one element there is nothing to choose. With two or three elements the multiplication table is essentially filled in by the cancellation laws, and you end up with the cyclic group every time. (You'll prove the prime-order case in general later — every group of prime order is cyclic.)

Order $4$ is the first place where genuine **choice** appears.

<Callout kind="example" title="Order 4">
There are exactly two groups of order $4$:
- $\Zc 4$, the cyclic group on four elements.
- $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 2$, called the **Klein four-group**.
</Callout>

These really are different. In $\Zc 4$ there is an element of order $4$ (the generator $\ol 1$). In $\Zc 2 \times \Zc 2$ every non-identity element has order $2$:

$$
(a, b) + (a, b) = (2a, 2b) = (0, 0).
$$

So no isomorphism can match them up — element orders are an isomorphism invariant.

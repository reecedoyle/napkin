<SlideLayout title="The definition of an isomorphism">

Let's promote the previous example to a general definition.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Isomorphism">
Let $G = (G, \star)$ and $H = (H, \ast)$ be groups. A bijection $\phi \colon G \to H$ is called an **isomorphism** if

$$
\phi(g_1 \star g_2) = \phi(g_1) \ast \phi(g_2) \qquad \text{for all } g_1, g_2 \in G.
$$

If there exists an isomorphism from $G$ to $H$, we say $G$ and $H$ are **<Term k="isomorphic">isomorphic</Term>** and write $G \cong H$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Two operations, one equation">
The two sides of the equation use *different* operations: the left-hand side $\phi(g_1 \star g_2)$ uses the operation $\star$ of $G$, while the right-hand side $\phi(g_1) \ast \phi(g_2)$ uses the operation $\ast$ of $H$. The whole point is that $\phi$ translates the structure of $G$ into the structure of $H$.
</Callout>

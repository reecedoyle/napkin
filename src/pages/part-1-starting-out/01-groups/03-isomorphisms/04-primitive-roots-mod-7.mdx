Here's a much less obvious isomorphism. The additive group $\Zc{6}$ and the multiplicative group $\Zm{7}$ both have $6$ elements — and in fact they are isomorphic as groups.

<Callout kind="example" title="Primitive roots modulo 7">
We claim $\Zc{6} \cong \Zm{7}$. The bijection is

$$
\phi(a \bmod 6) = 3^a \bmod 7.
$$

**Step 1: it's a bijection.** By direct calculation, working with the <Term k="congruent">congruence relation $\equiv$</Term>,

$$
(3^0, 3^1, 3^2, 3^3, 3^4, 3^5) \equiv (1, 3, 2, 6, 4, 5) \pmod 7.
$$

The right-hand side is exactly the six elements of $\Zm{7}$, each appearing once. So $\phi$ is a bijection from $\Zc{6}$ to $\Zm{7}$.

**Step 2: it respects the operation.** We need $\phi(a + b) = \phi(a) \cdot \phi(b)$, since $\Zc{6}$ uses addition while $\Zm{7}$ uses multiplication. Concretely,

$$
3^{(a + b) \bmod 6} \equiv 3^{a \bmod 6} \cdot 3^{b \bmod 6} \pmod 7,
$$

which is just the law of exponents (and the fact that $3^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$, so reducing the exponent mod $6$ is harmless).
</Callout>

So under $\phi$, *adding* in $\Zc{6}$ corresponds to *multiplying* in $\Zm{7}$ — two operations that look very different turn out to be the same group law in disguise.

</SlideLayout>

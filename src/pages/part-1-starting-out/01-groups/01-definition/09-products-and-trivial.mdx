We can also build new groups from old ones.

<Callout kind="example" title="Products of groups">
Let $(G, \star)$ and $(H, \ast)$ be groups. We can define a **product group** $(G \times H, \cdot)$ as follows. The elements of the group are ordered pairs $(g, h) \in G \times H$. The operation is
$$
(g_1, h_1) \cdot (g_2, h_2) = (g_1 \star g_2,\, h_1 \ast h_2) \in G \times H.
$$
</Callout>

<ProofReveal

  prompt="What are the identity element and the inverses of the product group G × H?"
  solution="The identity is the pair (1_G, 1_H), since (1_G, 1_H) · (g, h) = (1_G ⋆ g, 1_H ∗ h) = (g, h), and similarly on the right. The inverse of (g, h) is (g^{-1}, h^{-1}), where g^{-1} is the inverse of g in G and h^{-1} is the inverse of h in H. You can check: (g, h) · (g^{-1}, h^{-1}) = (g ⋆ g^{-1}, h ∗ h^{-1}) = (1_G, 1_H). Each component is just the corresponding group's structure, applied independently."

<Callout kind="example" title="Trivial group">
The **<Term k="trivial">trivial group</Term>**, often denoted $0$ or $1$, is the group with only an identity element. We will use the notation $\{1\}$.
</Callout>

<SlideLayout title="Non-examples of groups">

It's just as instructive to see what *isn't* a group.

<Callout kind="example" title="Non-examples">
- The pair <Term k="QQ">$(\QQ, \cdot)$</Term> is **not** a group. While there is an identity element, the element $0 \in \QQ$ does not have an inverse.
- The pair $(\ZZ, \cdot)$ is also **not** a group. (Why?)
- Let <Term k="Mat">$\Mat_{2 \times 2}(\RR)$</Term> be the set of $2 \times 2$ matrices with entries in the <Term k="RR">real numbers $\RR$</Term>. Then $(\Mat_{2 \times 2}(\RR), \cdot)$ (where $\cdot$ is matrix multiplication) is **not** a group. Indeed, even though we have an identity matrix
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix},
$$
we still run into the same issue: the zero matrix does not have a multiplicative inverse.

(Even if we delete the zero matrix from the set, the resulting structure is still not a group: those of you who know some linear algebra might recall that any matrix with determinant zero cannot have an inverse.)
</Callout>

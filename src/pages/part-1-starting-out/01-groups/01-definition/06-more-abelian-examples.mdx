Let's continue collecting examples. Here are more **abelian** groups.

<Callout kind="example" title="Complex unit circle">
Let <Term k="S1">$S^1$</Term> denote the set of <Term k="CC">complex numbers $\CC$</Term> $z$ with absolute value one; that is
$$
S^1 \defeq \{ z \in \CC \mid |z| = 1 \}.
$$
Then $(S^1, \times)$ is a group because

- The complex number $1 \in S^1$ serves as the identity, and
- Each $z \in S^1$ has an inverse $\frac{1}{z}$ which is also in $S^1$, since $|z\inv| = |z|\inv = 1$.

There is one thing we ought to check: that $z_1 \times z_2$ is actually still in $S^1$. But this follows from $|z_1 z_2| = |z_1| |z_2| = 1$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Addition mod n">
Let $n > 1$ be an integer, and consider the residues (remainders) modulo $n$. These form a group under addition, called the **cyclic group of order $n$**, denoted $\Zc{n}$, with elements <Term k="bar">$\ol{0}, \ol{1}, \dots$</Term>. The identity is $\ol{0}$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Multiplication mod p">
Let $p$ be a prime. Consider the *nonzero residues modulo $p$*, denoted <Term k="Zm">$\Zm{p}$</Term>: the set $\{\ol{1}, \ol{2}, \dots, \ol{p-1}\}$ under multiplication mod $p$. Then $(\Zm{p}, \times)$ is a group.
</Callout>

<SlideLayout title="Groups of prime order">

A short but powerful classification result. Once you've proved this, the only group of order $p$ — up to isomorphism — is $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$. So the moment you see $|G|$ is prime, you know *everything* about $G$ structurally.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why this matters">
This is the simplest non-trivial classification theorem in group theory. It also tells you why prime-order groups are "boring" — and why most of the interest in finite group theory lives at composite orders, where multiple non-isomorphic groups can coexist.
</Callout>

<Problem
  client:load
  id="grp-prob-prime-order"
  prompt="Let p be a prime. Show that if G is a group of order p, then G is isomorphic to Z/pZ."
  hint="Pick a non-identity element and look at the subgroup it generates. Then apply Lagrange's theorem."
  solution="Let G be a group of order p, and pick any element g ∈ G with g ≠ 1. Consider the cyclic subgroup H = ⟨g⟩ generated by g. By Lagrange's theorem, |H| divides |G| = p. Since p is prime, |H| is either 1 or p. But H contains both 1 and g ≠ 1, so |H| ≥ 2; therefore |H| = p, which means H = G.

So G is cyclic, generated by g, with |G| = p. The map Z/pZ → G sending k mod p to g^k is a well-defined isomorphism (well-defined because g^p = 1 by Lagrange; bijective because both sides have p elements and the map is surjective). Hence G ≅ Z/pZ."
  difficulty="starred"
/>

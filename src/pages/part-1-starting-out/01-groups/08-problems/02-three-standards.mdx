<SlideLayout title="Three standard problems">

Three short consolidations. The first re-derives Lagrange's theorem in the abelian case (a clean reuse of the Fermat's-little-theorem proof). The second pins down a small dihedral-vs-symmetric isomorphism — and a non-isomorphism. The third asks for an *explicit* embedding of $D_8$ inside $S_8$, foreshadowing Cayley's theorem.

<Problem
  client:load
  id="grp-prob-lagrange-abelian"
  prompt="Prove Lagrange's theorem for orders in the special case that G is a finite abelian group: that is, show g^|G| = 1 for every g in G."
  hint="Copy the proof of Fermat's little theorem, using the lemma that left-multiplication by g is a bijection of G."
  solution="Let n = |G| and write G = {g_1, g_2, ..., g_n}. For any fixed g in G, the map x ↦ gx is a bijection of G, so {g·g_1, g·g_2, ..., g·g_n} is the same set as {g_1, ..., g_n} — just reordered.

Take the product of all elements of G in two ways. On one hand it's g_1 · g_2 · ... · g_n. On the other, it's (g·g_1)(g·g_2)...(g·g_n). Because G is abelian we can pull all the g's to the front: that product equals g^n · (g_1 g_2 ... g_n).

So g^n · (g_1 g_2 ... g_n) = g_1 g_2 ... g_n. Cancelling the common factor (using the cancellation law) gives g^n = 1, as required."
  difficulty="standard"
/>

<Problem
  client:load
  id="grp-prob-d6-s3-vs-d24-s4"
  prompt="Show that D_6 is isomorphic to S_3, but D_24 is not isomorphic to S_4."
  hint="For the first part, decide where the isomorphism should send the generators r and s; the rest follows. For the second, look at element orders."
  solution="For D_6 ≅ S_3: D_6 is generated by a rotation r (of order 3) and a reflection s (of order 2), with sr = r^{-1} s. Send r to the 3-cycle (1 2 3) and s to the transposition (1 2). One checks the relations hold: (1 2 3) has order 3, (1 2) has order 2, and (1 2)(1 2 3) = (1 3 2) = (1 2 3)^{-1}. So this defines a homomorphism, and since both groups have order 6 and the map is surjective, it's an isomorphism. (The right-hand sides are written in cycle notation.)

For D_24 ≇ S_4: both groups have order 24, but D_24 contains the rotation r of order 12, whereas every element of S_4 has order at most 4 (the maximum cycle structure is a 4-cycle). Element orders are an isomorphism invariant, so the two groups can't be isomorphic."
  difficulty="standard"
/>

<Problem
  client:load
  id="grp-prob-d8-in-s8"
  prompt="A hint for Cayley's theorem. Find a subgroup H of S_8 which is isomorphic to D_8, and write the isomorphism explicitly."
  solution="Label the four vertices of a square 1, 2, 3, 4 and the four edge-midpoints 5, 6, 7, 8 (with 5 between 1–2, 6 between 2–3, 7 between 3–4, 8 between 4–1). Each symmetry of the square permutes these eight labels, giving an injection D_8 ↪ S_8.

Concretely: the rotation r by 90° corresponds to the permutation (1 2 3 4)(5 6 7 8), and the reflection s across the line through vertices 1 and 3 corresponds to (2 4)(6 8). The subgroup H ⊆ S_8 generated by these two permutations has order 8 and satisfies r^4 = s^2 = 1, srs = r^{-1} — the defining relations of D_8 — so H ≅ D_8.

(The trick generalises: think of each element g ∈ G as the permutation x ↦ gx of G itself. That gives an embedding G ↪ S_{|G|} for any finite G — which is exactly Cayley's theorem on the next slide but two.)"
  difficulty="standard"
/>

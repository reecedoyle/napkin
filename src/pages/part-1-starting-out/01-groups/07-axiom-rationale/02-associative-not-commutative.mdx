<SlideLayout title="Associative, but not commutative">

Two of our three axioms get singled out by this balancing act in opposite ways.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why associative is in">
Associativity is required because most of the operations we actually run into — addition of integers, multiplication of matrices, composition of functions — are associative. It also has a structural payoff: associativity is what forces the inverse of an element to be *unique*. Drop associativity and the theory of groups stops being recognisable.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why commutative is out">
Commutativity, on the other hand, is *not* required, even though many natural examples (the integers under addition, $\Zc n$, $\QQ^\times$) happen to satisfy it. The reason is simple: there are too many important groups that fail to commute — the symmetric groups $S_n$ for $n \geq 3$, the dihedral groups, $\GL_n(\RR)$, the quaternion group $Q_8$, and so on.

We don't want to legislate those away. So commutativity isn't an axiom — but because it's still useful to track, we give the commutative case its own name: a group whose operation commutes is called **abelian**.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

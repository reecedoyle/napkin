<SlideLayout title="Inverses and symmetry" part="Part I — Starting Out" chapter="1. Groups · Why these axioms?">

The third axiom — that every element has an inverse — is what gives a group its *symmetry*. Without it, some elements end up "bigger" than others.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why insist on inverses?">
Consider the natural numbers under addition: $(\NN, +)$. There is an identity element $0$, and the operation is associative, but this is **not** a group. The issue is that you can't subtract: there's no element $x \in \NN$ with $3 + x = 0$.

This asymmetry — some elements are "larger" than others, and the operation only ever moves you in one direction — is exactly what the inverse axiom rules out. With inverses, you can always undo any operation, and that gives the structure its uniformity.

(You also need the identity for this to even make sense: it's hard to say what an inverse *is* without something to invert *to*.)
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="A forward link: Cayley's theorem">
Later in this book, **Cayley's theorem** will make this rigorous. It says that every group $G$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of some symmetric group — in other words, every group can be realised as a group of permutations of some set. Groups, in this sense, *are* symmetries: the abstract axioms we wrote down turn out to capture exactly the structure of "things that act invertibly on a set".
</Callout>

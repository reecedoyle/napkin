A reasonable question to ask, having now worked with groups for a while, is: *why these axioms?* Why associative but not commutative? Why an inverse for every element?

The answer will only really click once you've seen more of the theory, but it's worth stopping to think about now.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The balancing act">
Whenever you define a new type of mathematical object, there is a tension to manage:

- **Too few constraints**, and your objects are wild — there's nothing interesting to prove about them.
- **Too many constraints**, and your definition only describes a handful of examples — the theory has nowhere to go.

The group axioms sit in a sweet spot: strict enough to force out beautiful theorems (Lagrange, Cayley, the classification of finite simple groups), loose enough to apply to integers, matrices, permutations, symmetries of a tile, roots of unity, and much more.
</Callout>

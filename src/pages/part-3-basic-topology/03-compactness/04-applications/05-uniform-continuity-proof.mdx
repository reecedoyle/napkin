<Callout kind="proposition" title="Continuous on compact ⟹ uniformly continuous">
If $M$ is <Term k="compact">compact</Term> and $f \colon M \to N$ is continuous, then $f$ is <Term k="uniformlyContinuous">uniformly continuous</Term>.
</Callout>

**Proof by contradiction (using sequences):** Fix $\varepsilon > 0$, and suppose uniform continuity fails. Then for every $\delta = 1/k$ there exist points $x_k, y_k \in M$ with $d(x_k, y_k) < 1/k$ but $d(f(x_k), f(y_k)) \ge \varepsilon$.

By compactness of $M$, extract a convergent subsequence $x_{i_k} \to p$. Since $d(x_{i_k}, y_{i_k}) < 1/i_k \to 0$, we also have $y_{i_k} \to p$.

By sequential continuity of $f$ at $p$, both $f(x_{i_k})$ and $f(y_{i_k})$ converge to $f(p)$. But we assumed $d(f(x_{i_k}), f(y_{i_k})) \ge \varepsilon > 0$ for all $k$ — a contradiction.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Sequentially compact metric space">
A metric space $M$ is <Term k="sequentiallyCompact">**sequentially compact**</Term> if every sequence in $M$ has a subsequence that converges to a point in $M$.
</Callout>

Both halves of the definition matter:

- **Has a convergent subsequence** — you don't need the whole sequence to converge, just some infinite sub-list.
- **Converges to a point in $M$** — the limit must actually be in the space. A sequence that "tries to converge" to a missing boundary point doesn't count.

The prototype is <Term k="compact">$[0,1]$</Term>: no matter how you scatter infinitely many points in $[0,1]$, some of them cluster together. The next slide examines the failures.

<SlideLayout title="Non-examples of compact spaces">

<Callout kind="example" title="Spaces that fail to be compact">
**(a) $\mathbb{R}$ is not compact.** The sequence $1, 2, 3, 4, \dots$ has no convergent subsequence — every subsequence diverges to $\infty$.

**(b) Unbounded spaces are never compact.** Any unbounded sequence has no convergent subsequence.

**(c) $(0,1)$ is not compact.** The sequence $\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{4}, \dots$ "converges to $0$", but $0 \notin (0,1)$. Every subsequence also converges to $0$, so there is no subsequence converging to a point inside $(0,1)$.

**(d) Incomplete spaces are never compact.** Any Cauchy sequence that fails to converge witnesses the failure. Completeness is necessary.
</Callout>

The pattern: to be compact you need two things:
1. **Bounded** — so sequences can't escape to infinity.
2. **Complete** — so Cauchy sequences don't converge to "missing" boundary points.

The Bolzano-Weierstraß theorem will make this precise for subsets of <Term k="RR">$\mathbb{R}^n$</Term>: compact $\iff$ closed and bounded.

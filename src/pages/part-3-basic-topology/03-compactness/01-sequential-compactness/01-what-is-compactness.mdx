<SlideLayout title="What is compactness?">

Compactness is one of the most important properties a topological space can have. Informally it captures the idea of being "closed and bounded" — but in a way that works for any topological space, not just subsets of <Term k="RR">$\mathbb{R}^n$</Term>.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Two roads to compactness">
For metric spaces there are two equivalent ways to define compactness:

1. **Sequential compactness** — every sequence has a convergent subsequence. Natural and hands-on.
2. **Open-cover compactness** — every open cover has a finite subcover. More abstract, but works for general topological spaces.

We start with the sequential version, then connect to the open-cover version.
</Callout>

Sequences in metric spaces behave well (they detect convergence, closure, continuity). The sequential definition takes advantage of this. For general topological spaces sequences become unreliable, so the open-cover definition is the one that travels.

<SlideLayout title="What doesn't carry over">

Not every metric concept has a topological analogue.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Completeness and boundedness are metric, not topological">
"Complete" and "(totally) bounded" are metric properties — they rely on the metric to state. They are not preserved by homeomorphism. The homeomorphism $(0,1) \cong \RR$ (the real line) shows this: $(0,1)$ is bounded and incomplete, while $\RR$ is unbounded and complete.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="remark" title="Sequences behave poorly in general topological spaces">
In metric spaces, a function is continuous iff it preserves convergent sequences. In a general topological space this fails. We therefore leave the notion of convergent sequences out of the purely topological setting; whenever sequences appear, we are working in a metric space.
</Callout>

The loss of sequences is a real cost — but it is exactly compensated by the extra generality we gain. Most spaces we care about in this course are either metric spaces or finite combinatorial objects, where the metric-space toolkit remains available.

</SlideLayout>

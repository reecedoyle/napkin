<SlideLayout title="Non-metrizable spaces">

Not every topological space is a metric space in disguise. A space homeomorphic to some metric space is called <Term k="metrizable">metrizable</Term>; many interesting spaces are not.

<Callout kind="example" title="A stupid non-metrizable space">
Take $X = \{a, b, c\}$ with topology $\mathcal{T} = \{\varnothing, X\}$. The only open sets are the empty set and $X$ itself. No metric on a three-point set can give this topology: a metric space must be able to separate any two distinct points with open balls of different sizes ($d(x,y) > 0$ for $x \neq y$), but this topology cannot separate $a$, $b$, and $c$ at all.
</Callout>

This motivates the <Term k="separationAxioms">separation axioms</Term>: a hierarchy of conditions that rule out such "stupid" topologies. The most common is the $T_2$ condition, which has a special name.

</SlideLayout>

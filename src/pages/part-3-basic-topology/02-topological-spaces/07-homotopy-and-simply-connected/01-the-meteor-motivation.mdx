<SlideLayout title="The meteor motivation">

In $\mathbb{C}$, all paths from $p$ to $q$ feel the same — you can continuously deform any one into any other.

Now imagine a meteor destroys the origin, leaving $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$. Suddenly there are two genuinely different paths from $p$ to $q$: one going to the left of the origin, one going to the right. You cannot continuously slide the "goes-left" path into the "goes-right" path without crossing the hole.

This is the motivating example for **homotopy**: the idea that two paths might or might not be deformable into each other while keeping endpoints fixed.

<Callout kind="remark">
This is closely related to winding numbers: the left path winds around the origin 0 times relative to the right path, and that difference is an obstruction to deforming one into the other.
</Callout>

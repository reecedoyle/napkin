<SlideLayout title="Basis of a topological space">

<Callout kind="definition" title="Basis">
A <Term k="basisTop">basis</Term> for a topological space $X$ is a collection $\mathcal{B}$ of open sets such that every open set in $X$ is a union of some elements of $\mathcal{B}$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Open intervals form a basis for ℝ">
The open intervals $(a,b)$ form a basis for $\RR$. Every open set is a union of such intervals.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="theorem" title="r-neighborhoods form a basis for any metric space">
In any metric space $M$, the open balls (r-neighborhoods) form a basis.
</Callout>

The proof: given an open set $U$ and any point $p \in U$, by definition of openness there is an open ball $B(p, r_p) \subseteq U$. Then $U = \bigcup_{p \in U} B(p, r_p)$ — a union of basis elements.

Hence in $\RR^2$, every open set is a union of open disks. We never need to worry about "exotic" open sets — just disks.

</SlideLayout>

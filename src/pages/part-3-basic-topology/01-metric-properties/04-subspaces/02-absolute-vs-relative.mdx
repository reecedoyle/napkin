<SlideLayout title="Absolute vs relative adjectives">

Subspaces force us to be careful about which adjectives are intrinsic ("absolute") and which require a parent space ("relative").

<Callout kind="definition" title="Absolute and relative adjectives">

- An **absolute adjective** (like "complete" or "bounded") applies to a metric space in isolation. Saying "$[0,1]$ is complete" or "$[0,1]$ is a complete subspace of $\mathbb{R}$" both make sense and mean the same thing.
- A **relative adjective** (like "<Term k="openSet">open</Term>" or "<Term k="closedSet">closed</Term>") only makes sense relative to a parent space. Asking "is $[0,1]$ open?" is meaningless on its own.

</Callout>

Relative adjectives need a parent:

- Is $[0,1]$ open **in $\mathbb{R}$**? No.
- Is $[0,1]$ open **in $[0,1]$**? Yes — every space is open in itself.
- Is $[0,\tfrac{1}{2})$ open **in $[0,1]$**? Yes — it's the open ball of radius $\tfrac{1}{2}$ centred at $0$ **within** $[0,1]$.
- Is $[0,\tfrac{1}{2})$ open **in $\mathbb{R}$**? No.

The moral: always specify the ambient space when you say "open" or "closed".

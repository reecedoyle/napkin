<SlideLayout title="Clopen sets and disconnected spaces">

A nontrivial set can be **both** open and closed — such sets are sometimes called **clopen** (with a knowing groan).

<Callout kind="example" title="A disconnected space">
Let $M = [0,1] \cup (2,3)$. Then $[0,1]$ is:

- <Term k="closedSet">closed in $M$</Term>: its complement $(2,3)$ is open in $M$.
- <Term k="openSet">open in $M$</Term>: every point of $[0,1]$ has a small ball (within $M$) entirely inside $[0,1]$, since $(2,3)$ is far away.

So $[0,1]$ is clopen in $M$. Symmetrically, $(2,3)$ is also clopen in $M$.
</Callout>

This is not a pathological accident — it's a sign that $M$ is **disconnected**: it splits into two pieces with a gap between them. The existence of a nontrivial clopen subset is precisely the topological definition of disconnectedness (which we'll formalise later).

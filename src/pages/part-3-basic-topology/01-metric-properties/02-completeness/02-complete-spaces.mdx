<SlideLayout title="Complete metric spaces">

A Cauchy sequence is trying to converge. Completeness says it succeeds.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Complete metric space">
A <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term> $M$ is **complete** if every <Term k="cauchySeq">Cauchy sequence</Term> in $M$ converges to a point of $M$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Complete spaces">

- <Term k="RR">$\mathbb{R}$</Term> is complete. (Depending on how you define $\mathbb{R}$, this is either the definition or a theorem.)
- The <Term k="discreteMetric">discrete metric space</Term> on any set is complete: the only Cauchy sequences are **eventually constant** (all terms equal from some index on), and those obviously converge.
- The closed interval $[0,1]$ is complete.
- <Term k="RRn">$\mathbb{R}^n$</Term> is complete.

</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Incomplete spaces">

- <Term k="QQ">$\mathbb{Q}$</Term> is **not** complete: the decimal approximations to $\sqrt{2}$ form a Cauchy sequence with no rational limit.
- The open interval $(0,1)$ is **not** complete: $0.9, 0.99, 0.999, \dots$ is Cauchy but converges to $1 \notin (0,1)$.

</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

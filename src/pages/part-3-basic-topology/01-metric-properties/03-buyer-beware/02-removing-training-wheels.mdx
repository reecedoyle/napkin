<SlideLayout title="Removing the metric training wheels">

We introduced metrics to make <Term k="openSet">open sets</Term> and <Term k="closedSet">closed sets</Term> concrete. Now we see the metric is carrying baggage those concepts don't need.

<Callout kind="remark">
Completeness and (total) boundedness are **metric properties** — they depend on the specific distance function, not just on which sets are open. Two homeomorphic metric spaces can differ on both. By contrast, properties like "every open cover has a finite subcover" (compactness) will turn out to be truly topological — preserved by homeomorphism.
</Callout>

The lesson:

- The metric is useful scaffolding — it gives us pictures of open balls and makes $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ arguments run.
- But the **open sets** derived from the metric are the real protagonist. They'll survive the transition to general topological spaces; the distances won't.

In the next chapter we'll define a topological space directly in terms of open sets, without any metric. The properties we study there — connectedness, compactness — will all be preserved by homeomorphism by definition.

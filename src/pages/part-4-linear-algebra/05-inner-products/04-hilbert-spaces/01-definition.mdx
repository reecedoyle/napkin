<SlideLayout title="Hilbert spaces — definition">

An <Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product space</Term> is a metric space (via $d(v,w) = \norm{v-w}$). A **Hilbert space** is one that is also complete.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Hilbert space">
A **<Term k="hilbertSpace">Hilbert space</Term>** is an inner product space $V$ whose corresponding metric space is <Term k="completeSpace">complete</Term> — every Cauchy sequence converges to a point of $V$.
</Callout>

In pure algebra we only allow finite linear combinations. But in a complete inner product space, infinite sums can sometimes converge, opening the door to analysis.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Finite-dimensional implies complete">
Every finite-dimensional inner product space is a Hilbert space. The idea: fix an orthonormal basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$. A Cauchy sequence in $V$ corresponds to $n$ Cauchy sequences of coordinates in $\RR$ (or $\CC$), and $\RR^n$ is complete.
</Callout>

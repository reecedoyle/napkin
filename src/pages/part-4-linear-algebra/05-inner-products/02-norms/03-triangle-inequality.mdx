<SlideLayout title="Triangle inequality and metric structure">

From Cauchy-Schwarz we get the triangle inequality, which makes $V$ a <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term>.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Triangle inequality">
For any $v, w$ in an inner product space,

$$\norm{v + w} \leq \norm{v} + \norm{w},$$

with equality if and only if $v$ and $w$ are linearly dependent and point in the same direction.
</Callout>

**Proof sketch.** Square both sides: the right side becomes $\norm{v}^2 + 2\norm{v}\norm{w} + \norm{w}^2$. The left side expands to $\norm{v}^2 + 2\,\Re\langle v, w \rangle + \norm{w}^2$. Since $\Re\langle v,w \rangle \leq |\langle v,w \rangle| \leq \norm{v}\norm{w}$ (by <Term k="cauchySchwarz">Cauchy-Schwarz</Term>), the inequality follows.

<Callout kind="remark" title="V becomes a metric space">
Define $d(v, w) = \norm{v - w}$. The triangle inequality shows this satisfies the metric axiom $d(v, w) \leq d(v, u) + d(u, w)$. And $d(v,w) = 0 \iff v = w$ by positive-definiteness. So every inner product space carries a natural <Term k="metricSpace">metric space</Term> structure.
</Callout>

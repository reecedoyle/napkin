<SlideLayout title="From dot products to inner products">

You already know the dot product in <Term k="RRn">$\RR^n$</Term>: for vectors $v = (a_1, \dots, a_n)$ and $w = (b_1, \dots, b_n)$,

$$v \cdot w = a_1 b_1 + \dots + a_n b_n.$$

Two things make this useful:

- $v \cdot w = 0$ exactly when $v$ and $w$ are **perpendicular**, and
- $|v|^2 = v \cdot v$.

The goal of this chapter is to export both ideas to any vector space over <Term k="RR">$\RR$</Term> or <Term k="CC">$\CC$</Term>. The resulting structure is called an **inner product**, and it gives the vector space a geometry — distances, angles, and a notion of orthogonality.

<Callout kind="remark" title="What we gain">
Once a vector space has an inner product, it becomes a metric space (via the norm $\|v\| = \sqrt{\langle v,v\rangle}$), and we can identify $V \cong V^\vee$ in a canonical way — something impossible with pure linear algebra.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

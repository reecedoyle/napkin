<Callout kind="example" title="The dot product is universal">
Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner product space and $e_1, \dots, e_n$ any <Term k="orthonormalBasis">orthonormal basis</Term>. For $v = \sum a_i e_i$ and $w = \sum b_j e_j$,

$$\langle v, w \rangle = \sum_{i,j} a_i \ol{b_j} \langle e_i, e_j \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \ol{b_i}.$$

The cross terms vanish because $\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = 0$ for $i \neq j$, and the diagonal terms give $1$ because $\norm{e_i} = 1$.
</Callout>

**Payoff.** The dot product formula $\sum a_i \ol{b_i}$ arises not because $\RR^n$ is special, but because the standard basis is orthonormal. Any inner product space, in any orthonormal basis, satisfies the same formula. Now you know why $\sum a_i b_i$ behaves so nicely.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Gram-Schmidt">
Every finite-dimensional <Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product space</Term> has an <Term k="orthonormalBasis">orthonormal basis</Term>.
</Callout>

The proof is constructive. Given any basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$, define $\operatorname{proj}_u(v) = \frac{\langle v, u \rangle}{\langle u, u \rangle}\, u$ and inductively subtract off the projections onto already-found basis vectors:

$$u_1 = e_1, \quad u_2 = e_2 - \operatorname{proj}_{u_1}(e_2), \quad u_3 = e_3 - \operatorname{proj}_{u_1}(e_3) - \operatorname{proj}_{u_2}(e_3), \quad \dots$$

Each $u_k$ is orthogonal to all previous $u_j$. Finally normalise: divide each $u_k$ by $\norm{u_k}$ to get unit vectors.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Why this matters">
Since every finite-dimensional inner product space has an orthonormal basis, we can always work in coordinates where the inner product looks like the standard dot product. This means the dot product formula $\langle v, w \rangle = \sum a_i \ol{b_i}$ is not special to $\RR^n$ — it is the universal form in any orthonormal basis.
</Callout>

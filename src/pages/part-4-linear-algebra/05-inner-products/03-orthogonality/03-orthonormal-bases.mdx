<SlideLayout title="Orthonormal bases">

<Callout kind="definition" title="Orthonormal basis">
An **<Term k="orthonormalBasis">orthonormal basis</Term>** of a finite-dimensional inner product space $V$ is a basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$ such that:
- $\norm{e_i} = 1$ for every $i$ (each vector has unit length), and
- $\langle e_i, e_j \rangle = 0$ for $i \neq j$ (distinct basis vectors are orthogonal).
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Standard orthonormal bases">
In $\RR^n$ and $\CC^n$ equipped with the standard dot product, the standard basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$ is orthonormal.
</Callout>

Working with an orthonormal basis simplifies everything: coordinates become inner products. If $v = \sum a_i e_i$ and $w = \sum b_i e_i$, then

$$\langle v, w \rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \ol{b_i},$$

which is exactly the dot product formula. This is no accident — the orthonormality forces it.

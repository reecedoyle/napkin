<SlideLayout title="The dual basis">

A linear map $V \to k$ is completely determined by what it does to a basis. This gives a canonical basis for <Term k="dualSpace">$V^\vee$</Term>.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Dual basis">
Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space with basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$. For each $i$, define $e_i^\vee \colon V \to k$ by

$$e_i^\vee(e_j) = \begin{cases} 1 & i = j \\ 0 & i \neq j \end{cases}$$

In words: $e_i^\vee(v)$ extracts the $e_i$-coefficient of $v$. Then $e_1^\vee, \dots, e_n^\vee$ is a basis of $V^\vee$, called the **<Term k="dualBasis">dual basis</Term>**.
</Callout>

**Concrete example:** With $V = \mathbb{R}^3$ and the standard basis, $e_1^\vee$ is the map $(x,y,z) \mapsto x$ — it reads off the first coordinate. Any linear functional $f = 3e_1^\vee + 4e_2^\vee + 5e_3^\vee$ satisfies:

$$f(e_1) = 3 \cdot 1 + 4 \cdot 0 + 5 \cdot 0 = 3 \qquad f(2,5,9) = 3 \cdot 2 + 4 \cdot 5 + 5 \cdot 9 = 71$$

This is exactly matrix multiplication by the row $[3 \; 4 \; 5]$.

</SlideLayout>

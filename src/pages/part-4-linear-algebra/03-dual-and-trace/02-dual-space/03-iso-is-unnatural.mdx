<SlideLayout title="V ≅ V∨ is not canonical">

Since $\dim V^\vee = \dim V$, one might declare $V \cong V^\vee$ via $e_i \mapsto e_i^\vee$. This is true as an abstract isomorphism — but it depends on the choice of basis.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The isomorphism V → V∨ is basis-dependent">
Alice picks basis $e_1 = (1,0,-1)$, $e_2 = (0,1,-1)$ for the plane $V = \{(x,y,z): x+y+z=0\}$. Bob picks $f_1 = (1,0,-1)$, $f_2 = (1,-1,0)$ for the same plane. Both construct "the" isomorphism $V \to V^\vee$ by sending each basis vector to its dual. But $A((1,0,-1))$ and $B((1,0,-1))$ are different elements of $V^\vee$: for $v = (3,5,-8)$, Alice's functional gives $3$ and Bob's gives $8$.
</Callout>

A "natural" isomorphism would not depend on the choice of basis. The map $e_i \mapsto e_i^\vee$ is natural only after you fix a basis. For inner product spaces, there is a truly canonical isomorphism — the subject of a later chapter.

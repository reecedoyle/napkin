<SlideLayout title="Hom as tensor — concrete example">

Let $V = \mathbb{R}^2$ with basis $e_1, e_2$ and let $T: V \to V$ be the map given by the matrix

$$T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

meaning $T(e_1) = e_1 + 3e_2$ and $T(e_2) = 2e_1 + 4e_2$.

The corresponding element of <Term k="dualSpace">$V^\vee$</Term> $\otimes V$ is:

$$e_1^\vee \otimes e_1 + 3e_1^\vee \otimes e_2 + 2e_2^\vee \otimes e_1 + 4e_2^\vee \otimes e_2$$

Wait — let's be careful: column $j$ of $T$ tells us $T(e_j)$. So:

$$\Psi^{-1}(T) = e_1^\vee \otimes (e_1 + 3e_2) + e_2^\vee \otimes (2e_1 + 4e_2)$$

<Callout kind="remark" title="The power of coordinate-free thinking">
Even though the expression $e_1^\vee \otimes e_1 + \cdots$ changes when you change the basis, the **actual element of $V^\vee \otimes V$ it describes is the same**. The tensor product captures what $T$ truly is, independent of coordinates.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="Hom space — the goal">

Linear maps $V \to W$ form a vector space — you can add two such maps or scale one. Let us give this space a name.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Hom space">
$\operatorname{Hom}(V, W)$ denotes the vector space of all linear maps from $V$ to $W$. When $V$ is $m$-dimensional and $W$ is $n$-dimensional, $\operatorname{Hom}(V,W)$ is $mn$-dimensional — think of it as the space of $n \times m$ matrices.
</Callout>

The moral of this section:

<Callout kind="remark" title="Key isomorphism">
There is a **natural isomorphism** $V^\vee \otimes W \cong \operatorname{Hom}(V, W)$.
</Callout>

Why does this make sense dimensionally? $\dim(V^\vee \otimes W) = \dim V^\vee \cdot \dim W = \dim V \cdot \dim W = \dim \operatorname{Hom}(V,W)$. The theorem says the correspondence is not just an abstract bijection — it is the canonical "God-given" one.

</SlideLayout>

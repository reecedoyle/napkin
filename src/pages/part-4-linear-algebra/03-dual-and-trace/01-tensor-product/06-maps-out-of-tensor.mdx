<SlideLayout title="Defining maps out of V ⊗ W">

To define a linear map $T \colon V \otimes W \to X$, it suffices to say what $T$ does to each <Term k="pureTensor">pure tensor</Term> $v \otimes w$. Since $V \otimes W$ is generated by pure tensors, linearity then determines $T$ on all sums.

<Callout kind="remark" title="The ⊗ wall is content-agnostic">
You can tensor *any* two $k$-vector spaces $V$ and $W$ — there is no required relationship between them. The $\otimes$ wall passes scalars, keeps elements of $V$ and $W$ separated, and remembers nothing else about the individual spaces.

In particular, even if $V = W$, we still have $v \otimes 1 \neq 1 \otimes v$ in general — just as $(g, 1_G) \neq (1_G, g)$ in the group $G \times G$.
</Callout>

This is why $\otimes$ is so powerful: by specifying what happens to pure tensors, you get a well-defined bilinear map, and the tensor product structure handles the rest.

</SlideLayout>

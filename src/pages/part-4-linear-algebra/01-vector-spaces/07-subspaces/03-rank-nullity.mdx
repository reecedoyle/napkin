<SlideLayout title="Rank-nullity theorem">

The most important result about linear maps is how dimension is shared between kernel and image.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Picking a basis for linear maps">
Let $T \colon V \to W$ be a linear map of finite-dimensional spaces, with $n = \dim V$, $m = \dim W$. There exist bases $e_1, \dots, e_n$ of $V$ and $f_1, \dots, f_m$ of $W$, and a nonneg integer $k$, such that $T(e_i) = f_i$ for $i \leq k$ and $T(e_i) = 0$ for $i > k$.
</Callout>

In the adapted bases, $T$ looks like: first $k$ basis vectors map bijectively to $f_1, \dots, f_k$; the remaining $n-k$ go to zero. So $\dim \ker T = n-k$ and $\dim T(V) = k$.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Rank-nullity theorem">
For $T \colon V \to W$ between finite-dimensional spaces:
$$\dim V = \dim \ker T + \dim T(V).$$
</Callout>

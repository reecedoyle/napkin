<SlideLayout title="Rings and fields — a quick review">

Linear algebra takes place inside a field. Before diving in, here is a compact reminder of the two structures involved.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Ring and field">
A **ring** is a structure with a commutative addition and multiplication (plus subtraction), additive identity <Term k="zeroR">$0$</Term> and multiplicative identity <Term k="oneR">$1$</Term>. A **field** <Term k="field">$k$</Term> is a ring in which every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse — you can always divide.
</Callout>

The prototype ring is <Term k="ZZ">$\ZZ$</Term>. The prototypical fields are <Term k="QQ">$\QQ$</Term>, <Term k="RR">$\RR$</Term>, and <Term k="CC">$\CC$</Term>. The integers fail to be a field because $2$ has no inverse in $\ZZ$.

**Convention.** The letter $R$ is reserved for a commutative ring; $k$ or $K$ for a field. If you replace "field" by "$\RR$" everywhere in this chapter, you lose almost nothing.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Additive abelian group">
An **additive abelian group** $(M, +)$ has a commutative addition, subtraction, and an identity $0$, but no required multiplication. The set $\RR^3$ with componentwise addition is a clean example.
</Callout>

<SlideLayout title="Splitting a space">

Go back to $V = \{ax^2 + bx + c \mid a, b, c \in \RR\}$. The three "components" $\RR x^2$, $\RR x$, and $\RR$ don't interact — every element of $V$ is uniquely a sum of one piece from each.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Direct sum (internal)">
Let $M$ be an $R$-module and $M_1, M_2$ submodules of $M$. We write $M = M_1 \oplus M_2$ and say $M$ is a <Term k="directSum">**direct sum**</Term> of $M_1$ and $M_2$ if every element of $M$ can be written **uniquely** as $m_1 + m_2$ with $m_1 \in M_1$ and $m_2 \in M_2$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="The Euclidean plane">
$\RR^2 = X \oplus Y$ where $X = \{(x, 0)\}$ and $Y = \{(0, y)\}$ are the $x$- and $y$-axes. Every point $(x, y)$ decomposes **uniquely** as $(x, 0) + (0, y)$.
</Callout>

So for our quadratic space:
$$V = \RR x^2 \oplus \RR x \oplus \RR.$$

Each summand is itself just a copy of $\RR$, so $V \cong \RR \oplus \RR \oplus \RR$.

</SlideLayout>

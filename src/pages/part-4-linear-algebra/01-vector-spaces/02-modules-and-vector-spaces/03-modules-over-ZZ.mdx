<SlideLayout title="Abelian groups as ℤ-modules">

Modules generalise vector spaces to any ring. Two important special cases:

<Callout kind="example" title="Every abelian group is a ℤ-module">
Any abelian group $(G, +)$ becomes a <Term k="rModule">$\ZZ$-module</Term> by defining

$$n \cdot g = \underbrace{g + \cdots + g}_{n \text{ times}} \quad \text{and} \quad (-n) \cdot g = n \cdot (-g)$$

for $n \geq 0$. In particular, $\ZZ/100\ZZ$ is a $\ZZ$-module: $n \cdot \bar{a} = \overline{na}$.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="example" title="Every ring is a module over itself">
Any commutative ring $R$ is an $R$-module over itself, using ring multiplication as the scalar multiplication. So $\RR$ is an $\RR$-module (= $\RR$-vector space), and $\ZZ$ is a $\ZZ$-module.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="question">
In the quadratic polynomial example, why must we say "degree **at most** 2" rather than "degree exactly 2"? In other words, why is $\{ax^2 + bx + c \mid a, b, c \in \RR,\ a \neq 0\}$ **not** a vector space?
</Callout>

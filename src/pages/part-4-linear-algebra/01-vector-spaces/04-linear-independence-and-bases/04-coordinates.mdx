<SlideLayout title="Coordinates and dimension">

Once you pick a <Term k="basis">basis</Term> $e_1, \dots, e_n$ of a vector space $V$, every vector $v \in V$ is uniquely $v = a_1 e_1 + \cdots + a_n e_n$. You can think of $v$ as the coordinate tuple $(a_1, \dots, a_n)$.

This shows $V \cong k^{\oplus n}$: pick any basis, and you get an isomorphism $v \mapsto (a_1, \dots, a_n)$. Different bases give different isomorphisms, but the number $n = \dim V$ is invariant.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Dimension">
The <Term k="dimension">**dimension**</Term> of $V$ over $k$, written $\dim_k V$ or $\dim V$, is the number of elements in any finite basis. We say $V$ is <Term k="finiteDim">**finite-dimensional**</Term> if such a basis exists; otherwise $V$ is **infinite-dimensional**.
</Callout>

Examples:
- $\dim_\RR \RR^3 = 3$.
- $\dim_\RR \CC = 2$ (basis $\{1, i\}$ over $\RR$).
- $\dim_\QQ \QQ[\sqrt{2}] = 2$ (basis $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$).
- $\RR[x]$ (all polynomials) is infinite-dimensional.

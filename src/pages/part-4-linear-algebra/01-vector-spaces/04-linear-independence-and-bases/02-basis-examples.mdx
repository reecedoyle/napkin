<Callout kind="example" title="Several bases">

- $\QQ[\sqrt{2}] = \{a + b\sqrt{2} \mid a, b \in \QQ\}$ as a $\QQ$-vector space has <Term k="basis">basis</Term> $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$.
- The space of all real polynomials has an **infinite** basis $\{1, x, x^2, \dots\}$ — finiteness of sums means polynomials have finite degree.
- $V = \{(x,y,z) \mid x+y+z=0\}$: there is no single "obvious" basis, but some working examples are $\{(1,-1,0),\ (1,0,-1)\}$ or $\{(0,1,-1),\ (1,0,-1)\}$.
</Callout>

What fails to be a basis? Take $S = \{3+x^2,\ x+1,\ 5+2x+x^2\}$ inside $V = \{ax^2+bx+c\}$:

1. **Not linearly independent**: $(3+x^2) + 2(x+1) - (5+2x+x^2) = 0$. A nontrivial combination equals zero.
2. **Not spanning**: $x^2$ cannot be written as a combination of elements of $S$.

A set is a basis iff it is **both** linearly independent and spanning.

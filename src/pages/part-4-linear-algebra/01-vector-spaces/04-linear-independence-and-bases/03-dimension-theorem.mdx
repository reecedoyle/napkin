<Callout kind="theorem" title="Maximality and minimality of bases">
Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $k$ and $e_1, \dots, e_n \in V$. The following are equivalent:

(a) The $e_i$ form a <Term k="basis">basis</Term>.
(b) The $e_i$ are spanning, but no proper subset is spanning.
(c) The $e_i$ are linearly independent, but adding any further element makes them dependent.
</Callout>

The equivalences (b) $\Rightarrow$ (a) and (c) $\Rightarrow$ (a) need the field structure: you must be able to divide by scalars. For a general module over a ring, (a) $\Rightarrow$ (b) and (a) $\Rightarrow$ (c) still hold, but the converses can fail.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Dimension theorem">
If a vector space $V$ has a finite basis, then **every** other basis has the same number of elements.
</Callout>

This lets us define the <Term k="dimension">**dimension**</Term> $\dim V$ as the size of any finite basis. For $V = \{ax^2+bx+c\}$, $\dim V = 3$.

<SlideLayout title="A tale of a non-canonical isomorphism">

This section is optional but instructive.
It shows what happens when you try to identify $V$ with $V^\vee$ without an inner product.

Given a basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$ of $V$, there is a "natural-looking" map $V \to V^\vee$ sending $e_i \mapsto e_i^\vee$.
But this isomorphism depends on the basis — it is **non-canonical**.
Abusing it leads to false theorems.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Row rank = column rank (a warm-up)">
Before the cautionary tale, note that the <Term k="dualMap">dual map</Term> already gives a clean proof:
by the transpose theorem, $\dim(\operatorname{im} T) = \dim(\operatorname{im} T^\vee)$.
The left side is the column rank of $M$; the right is the row rank.
So row rank equals column rank — no inner product needed.
</Callout>

The temptation: can we go further and use the non-canonical $V \cong V^\vee$ to prove things about $M^\top M$?

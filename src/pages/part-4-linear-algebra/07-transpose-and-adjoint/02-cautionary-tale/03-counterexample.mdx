<SlideLayout title="The counterexample">

The "false theorem" is indeed false over $\mathbb{C}$.

<Callout kind="exercise" title="Find a counterexample over ℂ">
Find a nonzero $2 \times 2$ complex matrix $M$ such that $M^\top M$ is the zero matrix.
</Callout>

**Answer:** take $M = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & i \\ i & -1 \end{bmatrix}$.
Then
$$
M^\top M = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & i \\ i & -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & i \\ i & -1 \end{bmatrix}
= \begin{bmatrix} 1 + i^2 & i - i \\ i - i & i^2 + 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
$$
So $M$ has rank 1 but $M^\top M$ has rank 0.

The flaw in the bogus proof: the non-canonical isomorphism $\iota \colon W \to W^\vee$ encodes as
some random invertible matrix $N$, so the composed map is $M^\top N M$, not $M^\top M$.
Over $\mathbb{R}$ with an inner product, $N$ can be forced to be the identity — but not over $\mathbb{C}$.

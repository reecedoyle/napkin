<SlideLayout title="Using the inner product to identify V with V∨">

Without an inner product, there is no natural isomorphism $V \cong V^\vee$.
With one, there is.

The idea: every vector $v \in V$ determines a <Term k="linearFunctional">linear functional</Term>
$\langle -, v \rangle \colon V \to k$, which sends $u \mapsto \langle u, v \rangle$.

**Example.** In $\mathbb{R}^3$ with orthonormal basis $e_1, e_2, e_3$, take $\xi \in V^\vee$ by
$\xi(e_1) = 3$, $\xi(e_2) = 4$, $\xi(e_3) = 5$.
Then $\xi(v) = \langle v, 3e_1 + 4e_2 + 5e_3 \rangle$ for every $v$.

So the vector $3e_1 + 4e_2 + 5e_3$ "represents" $\xi$ via the inner product.

<Callout kind="remark">
This correspondence — each vector $v$ representing the functional $\langle -, v \rangle$ — is
the **canonical** identification of $V$ with $V^\vee$ available when we have an
<Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product</Term>.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

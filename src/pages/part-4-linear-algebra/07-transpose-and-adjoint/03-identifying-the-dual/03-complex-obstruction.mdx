<SlideLayout title="Why the complex case is different">

Over $\mathbb{C}$, the same construction gives only a bijection, not a linear isomorphism.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Complex case: bijection but not isomorphism">
Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional **complex** <Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product space</Term>.
The map $v \mapsto \langle -, v \rangle$ is a bijection $V \to V^\vee$, but it is **not** a linear map.
</Callout>

The issue: the <Term k="sesquilinear">sesquilinear</Term> inner product is conjugate-linear in the second argument.
So
$$
iv \mapsto \langle -, iv \rangle = -i\langle -, v \rangle.
$$
Scaling by $i$ on the input produces $-i$ on the output — this is **anti-linear**, not linear.

The other natural candidate, $v \mapsto \langle v, - \rangle$, is not even in $V^\vee$:
$\langle v, - \rangle$ is anti-linear in its argument.

So over $\mathbb{C}$, no linear isomorphism $V \cong V^\vee$ arises from the inner product alone.
We will only need the bijection result in what follows.

</SlideLayout>

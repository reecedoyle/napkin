<SlideLayout title="Computing a dual map">

Let $V$ have basis $e_1, e_2, e_3$ and $W$ have basis $f_1, f_2$ (over $\mathbb{R}$).
Suppose
$$
T(e_1) = f_1 + 2f_2,\quad T(e_2) = 3f_1 + 4f_2,\quad T(e_3) = 5f_1 + 6f_2.
$$

The <Term k="dualBasis">dual bases</Term> are $e_1^\vee, e_2^\vee, e_3^\vee$ on $V^\vee$ and $f_1^\vee, f_2^\vee$ on $W^\vee$.
We compute $T^\vee(f_1^\vee) = f_1^\vee \circ T$:
$$
f_1^\vee(T(ae_1 + be_2 + ce_3)) = f_1^\vee((a+3b+5c)f_1 + (2a+4b+6c)f_2) = a + 3b + 5c.
$$
So $T^\vee(f_1^\vee) = e_1^\vee + 3e_2^\vee + 5e_3^\vee$.

Similarly $T^\vee(f_2^\vee) = 2e_1^\vee + 4e_2^\vee + 6e_3^\vee$.

<Callout kind="example" title="Matrices of T and T∨ are transposes">
In coordinates:
$$
T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 5 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
T^\vee = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \\ 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}.
$$
Each is a mirror image of the other over the diagonal — they are <Term k="transposeMatrix">transposes</Term>.
</Callout>

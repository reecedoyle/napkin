<SlideLayout title="The dual map">

Recall that the <Term k="dualSpace">dual space</Term> $V^\vee$ of a vector space $V$ is the space of all linear maps $V \to k$.
Each such map is called a <Term k="linearFunctional">linear functional</Term>.

Given a linear map $T \colon V \to W$, we can build a companion map that goes the other way on the dual spaces.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Dual map">
Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces and $T \colon V \to W$ a linear map.
The **dual map** (or **transpose map**)
$$
T^\vee \colon W^\vee \to V^\vee
$$
is defined by pre-composition: for any functional $f \in W^\vee$,
$$
T^\vee(f) = f \circ T.
$$
</Callout>

The direction reverses: $T$ goes $V \to W$, while <Term k="dualMap">$T^\vee$</Term> goes $W^\vee \to V^\vee$.
This reversal is the key feature.

Linearity of $T^\vee$ follows immediately: composing linear maps preserves linearity.

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="The spectral theorem">

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Normal ⟺ diagonalizable in an orthonormal basis">
Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional **complex** <Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product space</Term>.
A linear map $T \colon V \to V$ is <Term k="normalMap">normal</Term> if and only if there exists
an <Term k="orthonormalBasis">orthonormal basis</Term> of $V$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors of $T$.
</Callout>

This is sometimes called the **spectral theorem** (for normal operators on finite-dimensional spaces).

**Why this is surprising:** Recall Jordan form — even a map that is diagonalizable might not have an
*orthonormal* basis of eigenvectors. The theorem says normality is exactly the condition that forces this.

**Why the (⟸) direction is easy.** Write $T$ in the orthonormal eigenbasis; it becomes diagonal.
A diagonal matrix $D$ satisfies $D D^\dagger = D^\dagger D$ (both equal to the diagonal matrix of $|\lambda_i|^2$).

The hard direction is (⟹): normality forces diagonalizability, then the eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues
are automatically orthogonal.

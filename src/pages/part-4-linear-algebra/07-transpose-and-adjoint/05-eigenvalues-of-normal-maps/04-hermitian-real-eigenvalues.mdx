<Callout kind="theorem" title="Hermitian matrices have real eigenvalues">
Every <Term k="hermitian">Hermitian</Term> (self-adjoint) linear map $T \colon V \to V$ is diagonalizable,
and all of its eigenvalues are real.
</Callout>

<ProofReveal
  client:load
  id="trn-eigen-proof-real-evals"
  prompt="Assuming T is Hermitian (so T = T†), explain why all eigenvalues of T must be real."
  solution="Since T is Hermitian it is normal, so by the spectral theorem it has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors with eigenvalues λ₁, …, λₙ. In this basis T is diagonal with T = T† meaning each λᵢ = ̄λᵢ, which forces every λᵢ to be real."
/>

This is the advertised poster result for the chapter.
A symmetric real matrix (think: covariance matrices in statistics, the Laplacian in physics) is always diagonalizable with real eigenvalues — because it is self-adjoint over $\mathbb{R}$.

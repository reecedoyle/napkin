<SlideLayout title="Normal and Hermitian maps">

We now ask: when does a linear map $T \colon V \to V$ have an **orthonormal** basis of eigenvectors?

<Callout kind="definition" title="Normal, self-adjoint, Hermitian, symmetric">
Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear map on a finite-dimensional inner product space.

- $T$ is **normal** if $TT^\dagger = T^\dagger T$ (the map commutes with its adjoint).
- $T$ is **self-adjoint** or <Term k="hermitian">**Hermitian**</Term> (over $\mathbb{C}$) if $T = T^\dagger$.
- For a real inner product space, $T = T^\dagger$ also goes by **symmetric**.
</Callout>

Examples of normal maps:
- Every Hermitian (self-adjoint) map is normal (since $T = T^\dagger$ trivially commutes with itself).
- Diagonal matrices (in any orthonormal basis) are normal.
- Unitary matrices $U$ (with $U^\dagger U = UU^\dagger = I$) are normal.

Non-example: upper triangular matrices with non-zero off-diagonal entries are generally **not** normal.

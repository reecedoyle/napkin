<Callout kind="theorem" title="Adjoints are conjugate transposes">
Fix an <Term k="orthonormalBasis">orthonormal basis</Term> of a finite-dimensional inner product space $V$.
Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear map with matrix $[a_{ij}]$ in this basis.
Then the matrix of $T^\dagger$ (in the same basis) has $(i,j)$ entry $\overline{a_{ji}}$ — the
<Term k="conjugateTranspose">conjugate transpose</Term> of the matrix of $T$.
</Callout>

**Proof.** Take $v = e_i$ and $w = e_j$ in the defining equation $\langle v, T^\dagger(w) \rangle = \langle T(v), w \rangle$.
The right side is $\langle T(e_i), e_j \rangle = a_{ji}$.
The left side is $\langle e_i, T^\dagger(e_j) \rangle$, which extracts the $i$-th coefficient of $T^\dagger(e_j)$.
Accounting for sesquilinearity, that coefficient is $\overline{a_{ji}}$.

In matrix notation: if $T$ has matrix $A$, then $T^\dagger$ has matrix $A^\dagger = \overline{A^\top}$.

Over $\mathbb{R}$: $A^\dagger = A^\top$ (conjugation does nothing to real numbers).

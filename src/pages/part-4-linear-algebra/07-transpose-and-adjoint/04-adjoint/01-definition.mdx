<SlideLayout title="The adjoint map">

For inner product spaces, the "right" companion to $T$ is not the dual map $T^\vee$ but the **adjoint** $T^\dagger$ — defined entirely within $V$ and $W$, without invoking dual spaces.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Adjoint">
Let $V$ and $W$ be finite-dimensional <Term k="innerProductSpace">inner product spaces</Term> and $T \colon V \to W$ a linear map.
The **adjoint** (or **conjugate transpose**) of $T$, written $T^\dagger \colon W \to V$, is defined by:
for every $w \in W$, $T^\dagger(w)$ is the unique vector in $V$ satisfying
$$
\langle v, T^\dagger(w) \rangle_V = \langle T(v), w \rangle_W \quad \text{for all } v \in V.
$$
</Callout>

**Why $T^\dagger(w)$ is well-defined.** The map $v \mapsto \langle T(v), w \rangle_W$ is a linear functional on $V$.
By the bijection $V \to V^\vee$ (from $\langle -, - \rangle$), this functional equals $\langle -, u \rangle_V$ for
a unique $u \in V$. That $u$ is <Term k="adjointMap">$T^\dagger(w)$</Term>.

The definition involves no basis and no dual spaces — just the inner product.

</SlideLayout>

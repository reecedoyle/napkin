This section is **optional** — you can skip it and still follow the rest of the chapter. It fills in the proof that every nilpotent map is a direct sum of staircases.

**Goal.** Given nilpotent $T \colon V \to V$ (finite-dimensional), build a basis of $V$ consisting of staircases.

**Proof by induction on $\dim V$.** The base case $\dim V = 0$ is trivial. Assume $\dim V \geq 1$.

Let $W = T(V)$ be the image of $T$. Since $T$ is nilpotent, $W \subsetneq V$ (the image is strictly smaller). If $W = \{0\}$ then $T$ is the zero map and every basis vector is a length-$1$ staircase — done.

So assume $\{0\} \subsetneq W \subsetneq V$.

**Inductive hypothesis.** Since $\dim W < \dim V$, the restriction $T|_W \colon W \to W$ is also nilpotent, and by induction $W$ already has a staircase basis:

$$
\mathcal{B}' = \bigl\{ T(v_1), T^2(v_1), \dots;\; T(v_2), T^2(v_2), \dots;\; \dots;\; T(v_\ell), T^2(v_\ell), \dots \bigr\}
$$

for some $v_1, \dots, v_\ell \in V$ (each element of $W$ is of the form $T(v)$ for some $v$, so we can choose the $v_i$).

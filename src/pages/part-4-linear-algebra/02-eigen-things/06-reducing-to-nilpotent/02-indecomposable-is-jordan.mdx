<SlideLayout title="Each indecomposable piece is a Jordan block">

The payoff: every indecomposable $T$-invariant piece of $V$ is a single Jordan block.

**Argument.** Take an indecomposable summand $V_1$ from the decomposition. Since the base field is algebraically closed, $T \colon V_1 \to V_1$ has an eigenvalue $\lambda_1$. Let $S = T - \lambda_1 \id$, so $\ker S \neq \{0\}$.

A standard lemma (that we quote) gives, for large enough $N$:

$$
V_1 = \ker S^N \oplus \operatorname{im} S^N.
$$

Since $T$ (hence $S$) is indecomposable on $V_1$ and $\ker S^N$ is nontrivial (it contains eigenvectors), we must have $\operatorname{im} S^N = \{0\}$, i.e. $S^N = 0$. So $S = T - \lambda_1 \id$ is nilpotent on $V_1$.

By Nilpotent Jordan, $V_1$ decomposes into staircases under $S$. But $T$ is indecomposable on $V_1$, so there is only **one** staircase. One staircase = one Jordan block.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Summary">
The Jordan form theorem follows by combining two facts:
1. Every $V$ decomposes into indecomposable $T$-invariant pieces.
2. Every indecomposable piece is a single Jordan block (because $T - \lambda \id$ is nilpotent there, and the piece is indecomposable).
</Callout>

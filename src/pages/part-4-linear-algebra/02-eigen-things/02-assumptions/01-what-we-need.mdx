<SlideLayout title="Assumptions — what we need and why">

Most big theorems in this chapter require two hypotheses. It is worth spelling them out before we dive in.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Standing assumptions">
Theorems (but not definitions) in this chapter require:

- $V$ is a **finite-dimensional** vector space.
- The base field $k$ is **algebraically closed** — every polynomial over $k$ has a root in $k$.
</Callout>

**Why algebraically closed?** The prototype is <Term k="CC">$\CC$</Term>. The fundamental theorem of algebra says every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has a complex root. That is exactly the algebraically closed condition.

**Why does it matter?** Eigenvalues are roots of a certain polynomial attached to $T$. If the field is not algebraically closed, that polynomial might have no roots — meaning no eigenvalues exist. The rotation of $\RR^2$ by $90°$ is the classical example: no real eigenvector, but over $\CC$ there are two.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Practical consequence">
Any real matrix can be viewed as a complex matrix whose entries happen to be real. So working over $\CC$ is not a real restriction in practice — it just guarantees the theory is complete.
</Callout>

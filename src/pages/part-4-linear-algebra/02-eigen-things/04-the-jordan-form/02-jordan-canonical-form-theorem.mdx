Here is the main result of the chapter.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Jordan canonical form">
Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear map of finite-dimensional vector spaces over an algebraically closed field $k$. Then there exists a basis of $V$ in which the matrix of $T$ is **block-diagonal**, with each block a Jordan block.

Such a matrix is said to be in <Term k="jordanForm">**Jordan form**</Term>. This form is unique up to reordering the blocks.
</Callout>

Concretely, the matrix looks like:

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_1 & 1 & & & & \\
0 & \lambda_1 & & & & \\
& & \lambda_2 & & & \\
& & & \lambda_3 & 1 & 0 \\
& & & 0 & \lambda_3 & 1 \\
& & & 0 & 0 & \lambda_3 \\
& & & & & & \ddots
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The big picture: $V$ decomposes as a direct sum $V = J_1 \oplus J_2 \oplus \dots \oplus J_m$, and $T$ acts on each subspace $J_i$ independently. In the simplest case each $J_i$ is one-dimensional — a pure eigenvector. When a block has size $> 1$, there are $1$s above the diagonal, indicating that the map is "almost" scaling but not quite.

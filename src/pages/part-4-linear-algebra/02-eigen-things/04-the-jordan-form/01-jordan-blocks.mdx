A diagonal matrix is the ideal case. When diagonalisation is impossible, the next best thing is a <Term k="jordanBlock">**Jordan block**</Term>.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Jordan block">
A **Jordan block** is an $n \times n$ matrix of the form

$$
J_\lambda =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda & 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & \lambda & 1 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \lambda & \dots & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & \lambda
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

It has $\lambda$ on the main diagonal and $1$ just above it; all other entries are $0$. A $1 \times 1$ Jordan block is just $\begin{bmatrix}\lambda\end{bmatrix}$.
</Callout>

Note that a diagonal matrix is the special case where every Jordan block has size $1 \times 1$ — there are no $1$s above the diagonal. The larger the block, the more the map deviates from pure scaling at eigenvalue $\lambda$.

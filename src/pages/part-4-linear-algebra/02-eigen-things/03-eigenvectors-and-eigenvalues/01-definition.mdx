<SlideLayout title="Eigenvectors and eigenvalues — definition">

The "nice" vectors we spotted in the previous section — those that $T$ merely scales — deserve a name.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Eigenvector and eigenvalue">
Let $T \colon V \to V$ and let $v \in V$ be a **nonzero** vector.

We call $v$ an <Term k="eigenvector">**eigenvector**</Term> of $T$ if $T(v) = \lambda v$ for some scalar $\lambda \in k$.

The scalar $\lambda$ is called an <Term k="eigenvalue">**eigenvalue**</Term> of $T$.

We sometimes say "$v$ is a $\lambda$-eigenvector" as shorthand.
</Callout>

Key points:
- $v$ must be **nonzero**. The zero vector satisfies $T(0) = 0 = \lambda \cdot 0$ for every $\lambda$, so it is vacuously an eigenvector for any eigenvalue — that is why we exclude it.
- $\lambda$ itself can be zero (provided $v \neq 0$).
- No basis is mentioned — eigenvectors are intrinsic to the map, not to any particular choice of coordinates.

<SlideLayout title="Shifting the eigenvalue">

Here is the key trick connecting nilpotent maps to Jordan blocks with arbitrary eigenvalue $\lambda$.

Take the double staircase matrix $S$ (eigenvalue $0$) from the previous slide. Add $\lambda \cdot \id$:

$$
S + \lambda \id =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda & 1 & 0 &   &   \\
0 & \lambda & 1 &   &   \\
0 & 0 & \lambda &   &   \\
  &   &   & \lambda & 1 \\
  &   &   & 0 & \lambda
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

That is a pair of Jordan blocks with eigenvalue $\lambda$ — exactly Jordan form!

<Callout kind="remark" title="The plan">
To prove Jordan form for a general $T$:
1. Break $V$ into subspaces on each of which $T - \lambda \id$ is nilpotent (for the right $\lambda$).
2. Apply Nilpotent Jordan to each subspace to get the individual Jordan blocks.
</Callout>

Step 1 is the content of the next section ("Reducing to the nilpotent case"). Step 2 is already done by the Nilpotent Jordan theorem.

<SlideLayout title="The simplest linear maps">

A square matrix $T$ is really just a linear map from a vector space $V$ to itself. What is the **simplest** kind of such map?

The very simplest: multiply every vector by the same scalar $\lambda$.

$$
T =
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda & 0 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & \lambda & \dots & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & \dots & \lambda
\end{bmatrix}
$$

That is a bit too simple — every direction behaves identically. A richer but still manageable situation: scale each **basis element** $e_i$ by its own scalar $\lambda_i$.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Diagonal matrix">
A <Term k="diagonalMatrix">**diagonal matrix**</Term> is one that has the form

$$
T = \begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\
0 & \lambda_2 & \dots & 0 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & \dots & \lambda_n
\end{bmatrix}
$$

where $e_i \mapsto \lambda_i e_i$ for each basis element $e_i$.
</Callout>

Diagonal maps are extremely convenient. For example, $T^{100}$ is immediate: it sends $e_i \mapsto \lambda_i^{100} e_i$. Try that with an arbitrary $n \times n$ matrix.

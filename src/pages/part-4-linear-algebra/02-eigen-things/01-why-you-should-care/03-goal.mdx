<SlideLayout title="The goal — Jordan form">

Our dream: every linear map $T \colon V \to V$ is diagonal in some basis. That turns out to be **too optimistic** — some maps genuinely resist diagonalisation.

The good news: every map over an algebraically closed field is *almost* diagonal. The precise statement is the <Term k="jordanForm">Jordan canonical form theorem</Term>, which we'll build toward in this chapter.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Road map">
We will:
1. Define **eigenvectors** and **eigenvalues** — the special vectors that *are* scaled by $T$.
2. Define **Jordan blocks** — the unavoidable near-diagonal pieces.
3. Prove that every map breaks into Jordan blocks (via **nilpotent maps**).
4. Refine with the notions of **algebraic** and **geometric multiplicity**.
</Callout>

Two assumptions will underpin most theorems: $V$ must be **finite-dimensional**, and the base field must be **algebraically closed** (e.g. $\CC$). The definitions work without these, but the big theorems need them.

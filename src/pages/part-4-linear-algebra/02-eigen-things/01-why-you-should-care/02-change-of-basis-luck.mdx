<SlideLayout title="Getting lucky — a hidden diagonal">

Most linear maps do not look diagonal in the standard basis. But the **same** map can look diagonal in a better basis.

<Callout kind="example" title="Getting lucky">
Let $V$ be two-dimensional with basis $e_1, e_2$, and define $T$ by

$$
e_1 \mapsto 2e_1, \qquad e_2 \mapsto e_1 + 3e_2.
$$

In the basis $e_1, e_2$ the matrix is $\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ — not diagonal. But notice:

$$
e_1 \mapsto 2e_1, \qquad e_1 + e_2 \mapsto 3(e_1 + e_2).
$$

Switch to the basis $\{e_1,\, e_1 + e_2\}$. In this new basis,

$$
T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}.
$$

Diagonal after all!
</Callout>

The point: a "random-looking" map can become the very nice <Term k="diagonalMatrix">diagonal form</Term> once we choose the right basis. This chapter is about **making** that luck systematic.

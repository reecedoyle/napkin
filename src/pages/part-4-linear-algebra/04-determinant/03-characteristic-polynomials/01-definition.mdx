<SlideLayout title="Characteristic polynomial — definition">

Over an algebraically closed field, every linear map has eigenvalues. Packaging them into a polynomial is useful.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Characteristic polynomial">
Let $T \colon V \to V$ be a linear map on an $n$-dimensional vector space over an algebraically closed field, with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ (counted with algebraic multiplicity). The **characteristic polynomial** of $T$ is

$$
p_T(X) = (X - \lambda_1)(X - \lambda_2) \cdots (X - \lambda_n).
$$
</Callout>

If $T$ is written in Jordan normal form (upper-triangular with eigenvalues on the diagonal), this polynomial appears naturally as

$$
p_T(X) = \det(X \cdot \mathrm{id} - T).
$$

That determinant definition is what most linear algebra books use — it avoids mentioning eigenvalues directly. But defining $p_T$ in terms of eigenvalues is more transparent, and it makes the next theorem almost obvious.

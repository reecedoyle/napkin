<SlideLayout title="Cayley–Hamilton — proof via Jordan form">

The proof uses the Jordan decomposition of $V$.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Setup">
Write $V = J_1 \oplus J_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus J_m$ in Jordan normal form, where $J_i$ is a generalised eigenspace for eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ with dimension $d_i$.

Then by definition:

$$
p_T(X) = (X - \lambda_1)^{d_1}(X - \lambda_2)^{d_2} \cdots (X - \lambda_m)^{d_m}.
$$
</Callout>

<ProofReveal
  client:load
  id="det-ch-proof-cayley-hamilton"
  prompt="Using the Jordan decomposition, prove that p_T(T) is the zero map."
  solution="Evaluate p_T(T) = (T − λ₁·id)^{d₁} ∘ (T − λ₂·id)^{d₂} ∘ ··· ∘ (T − λₘ·id)^{dₘ}. By definition of the generalised eigenspace J₁, the map (T − λ₁·id)^{d₁} is the zero map on J₁. So p_T(T) is zero on J₁. By the same argument it is zero on each Jᵢ. Since V = J₁ ⊕ ··· ⊕ Jₘ, p_T(T) is the zero map on all of V."
/>

The key insight: the factor $(T - \lambda_i \cdot \mathrm{id})^{d_i}$ annihilates $J_i$ by the very definition of the generalised eigenspace. So the product of all factors annihilates each summand, hence the whole space.

</SlideLayout>

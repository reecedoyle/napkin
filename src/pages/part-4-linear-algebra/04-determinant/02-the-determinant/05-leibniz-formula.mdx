<SlideLayout title="The Leibniz formula">

Expanding the wedge product of the column vectors of a matrix yields the classical formula.

<Callout kind="theorem" title="Leibniz formula">
For an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ with entries $a_{ij}$,

$$
\det(A) = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)\, a_{1,\sigma(1)}\, a_{2,\sigma(2)} \cdots a_{n,\sigma(n)}.
$$
</Callout>

**Where it comes from.** Let $T(e_j) = \sum_i a_{ij} e_i$ (the $j$-th column of $A$ expressed in the basis). Then

$$
T(e_1) \wedge \cdots \wedge T(e_n) = \Bigl(\sum_{i} a_{i1} e_i\Bigr) \wedge \cdots \wedge \Bigl(\sum_{i} a_{in} e_i\Bigr).
$$

Expanding by multilinearity gives $n^n$ terms, but almost all vanish: we need to pick one basis vector from each factor, and if any two choices are equal the wedge is zero. So we sum over all ways to pick distinct indices — i.e., over permutations $\sigma$. The anti-symmetry brings in the sign $\operatorname{sgn}(\sigma)$.

<Callout kind="remark">
American high-school students learn this as the *definition* of the determinant. It is actually a corollary of the wedge-product definition, and should be viewed as a computational tool rather than a conceptual starting point.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

<SlideLayout title="The top wedge is one-dimensional">

Here is the key observation that makes the determinant possible.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="∧ⁿ(V) is one-dimensional">
If $\dim V = n$, then $\dim \bigwedge^n(V) = \binom{n}{n} = 1$.
</Callout>

A **one-dimensional** vector space over $k$ is isomorphic to $k$ itself. In particular, every linear map from a one-dimensional space to itself is just **multiplication by a scalar**.

So $\bigwedge^n(T) \colon \bigwedge^n(V) \to \bigwedge^n(V)$ is multiplication by some scalar $c \in k$.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Determinant">
Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space and $T \colon V \to V$ a linear map. The **determinant** $\det T$ is the unique scalar $c$ such that

$$
\bigwedge^n(T) = c \cdot \mathrm{id}_{\bigwedge^n(V)}.
$$

Concretely, if $e_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge e_n$ is any nonzero element of $\bigwedge^n(V)$, then

$$
T(e_1) \wedge \cdots \wedge T(e_n) = (\det T)(e_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge e_n).
$$
</Callout>

The definition uses no choice of basis for $V$ — it only uses the linear map $T$ and the canonical construction $\bigwedge^n$. So the determinant is **basis-independent by construction**.

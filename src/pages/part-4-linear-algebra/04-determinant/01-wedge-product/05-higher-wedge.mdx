<SlideLayout title="The higher wedge product ∧ᵐ(V)">

The same idea extends from pairs to $m$-tuples.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Higher wedge product ∧ᵐ(V)">
For a vector space $V$ and positive integer $m$, the space $\bigwedge^m(V)$ is generated by symbols $v_1 \wedge v_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge v_m$ subject to:

- **Multilinearity** in each slot separately.
- **Vanishing**: $\dots \wedge v \wedge v \wedge \dots = 0$ whenever the same $v$ appears twice (in adjacent positions — the other cases follow).
- **Anti-symmetry**: swapping any two adjacent entries negates the whole wedge.
</Callout>

As a consequence, any wedge with a *repeated* vector is zero — not just adjacent repeats. You can see this by swapping the two equal entries: swapping changes the sign, but equal entries means nothing changed, so $0 = $ itself $+ $ itself, i.e., $= 0$.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Basis of ∧ᵐ(V)">
If $V$ has basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$, then $\bigwedge^m(V)$ has basis

$$
\{ e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_m} : 1 \le i_1 < i_2 < \cdots < i_m \le n \}.
$$

Hence $\dim \bigwedge^m(V) = \binom{n}{m}$.
</Callout>

When $m = n$, we get $\binom{n}{n} = 1$: the top wedge is **one-dimensional**. That single dimension is the key to defining the determinant.

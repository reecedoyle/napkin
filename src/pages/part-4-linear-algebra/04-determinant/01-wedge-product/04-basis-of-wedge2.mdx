Now we know what $\bigwedge^2(V)$ looks like when $V$ has a basis.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Basis of ∧²(V)">
Let $V$ be an $n$-dimensional vector space with basis $e_1, \dots, e_n$. A basis for $\bigwedge^2(V)$ is

$$
\{ e_i \wedge e_j : 1 \le i < j \le n \}.
$$

In particular, $\dim \bigwedge^2(V) = \binom{n}{2}$.
</Callout>

**Why?** Any generator $v \wedge w$ can be expanded into these basis elements using bilinearity. The anti-symmetry relation $e_j \wedge e_i = -(e_i \wedge e_j)$ lets us always put $i < j$, and $e_i \wedge e_i = 0$ removes all diagonal terms.

  prompt="What is the dimension of ∧²(ℝ⁴)?"
  expected="6"

For $V = \mathbb{R}^4$ (with $n = 4$), $\binom{4}{2} = 6$. The six basis elements are $e_1 \wedge e_2$, $e_1 \wedge e_3$, $e_1 \wedge e_4$, $e_2 \wedge e_3$, $e_2 \wedge e_4$, $e_3 \wedge e_4$.

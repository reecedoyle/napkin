<SlideLayout title="Summary table — flavors of Fourier analysis">

All three examples fit the same template: pick a domain $Z$, give it an inner product, find an orthonormal basis of characters. Here is the map:

| Type | Physical variable | Frequency variable | Characters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Binary | $\{\pm1\}^n$ | Subsets $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}$ | $\prod_{s\in S} x_s$ |
| Finite group | $Z$ | $\xi \in Z$ (with choice of $\cdot$) | $e(\xi \cdot x)$ |
| Fourier series | $[-\pi,\pi]$ | $n \in \ZZ$ | $\exp(inx)$ |
| Discrete | $\Zc{n}$ | $\xi \in \Zc{n}$ | $e(\xi x / n)$ |

The discrete row ($Z = \Zc{n}$) is a special case of the finite-group row.

<Callout kind="remark" title="Binary analysis is a special case of finite groups">
The group $\{\pm1\}^n$ under pointwise multiplication is isomorphic to $(\ZZ/2\ZZ)^n$. With the right dot product, the characters $e_\xi$ coincide with the monomials $\chi_S$. Binary Fourier analysis is subsumed by finite-group Fourier analysis.
</Callout>

</SlideLayout>

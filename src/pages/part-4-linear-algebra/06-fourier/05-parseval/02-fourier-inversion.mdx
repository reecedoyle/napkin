<SlideLayout title="Fourier inversion formula">

The orthonormal-basis fact $a_\xi = \langle x, e_\xi \rangle$ says: to extract the $\xi$-th coordinate, take the inner product with $e_\xi$.

<Callout kind="corollary" title="Fourier inversion formula">
**Finite abelian group:** For $f \colon Z \to \CC$,

$$\wh{f}(\xi) = \frac{1}{|Z|} \sum_{x \in Z} f(x)\,\overline{e_\xi(x)}.$$

**Fourier series:** For square-integrable $f \colon [-\pi,\pi] \to \CC$,

$$\wh{f}(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\,\exp(-inx)\, dx.$$
</Callout>

*Proof:* $\wh{f}(\xi) = \langle f, e_\xi \rangle$, which by definition of the inner product gives the formulas above. (In the series case: $\overline{\exp(inx)} = \exp(-inx)$.)

<Callout kind="question">
What does the inversion formula give when $\xi = 0$? For a finite group: $\wh{f}(0) = \frac{1}{|Z|}\sum_x f(x)$ — the average value. For $L^2$: $\wh{f}(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int f(x)\,dx$ — also the average. The zero frequency always captures the mean.
</Callout>

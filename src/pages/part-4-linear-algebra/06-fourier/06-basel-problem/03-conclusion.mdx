Putting it all together:

$$\sum_{n \in \ZZ} |\wh{f}(n)|^2 = |\wh{f}(0)|^2 + \sum_{n \neq 0} \frac{1}{n^2} = 0 + 2\sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^2}.$$

By Parseval this equals $\frac{\pi^2}{3}$, so:

$$2\sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{3} \implies \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}.$$

<Callout kind="remark" title="What just happened?">
Euler proved this in 1734 by ingenious polynomial tricks. The Fourier proof is arguably cleaner: pick the right function, apply Parseval, and the sum drops out of a calculus computation. No cleverness about zeros of sine required.
</Callout>

The same technique works for $\sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{1}{n^4}$ using $f(x) = x^2$ — that is Problem 1 at the end of the chapter.

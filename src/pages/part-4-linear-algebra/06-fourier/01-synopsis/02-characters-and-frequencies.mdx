The key move is choosing a *good* orthonormal basis for the function space.

<Callout kind="definition" title="Characters and frequencies">
An **orthonormal basis** $(e_\xi)_\xi$ of the function space is called the **characters**. The indexing set — the variable $\xi$ — is called the set of **frequencies**. The variable $x \in Z$ on which $f$ is evaluated is the **physical variable**.
</Callout>

Once we have such a basis, every function $f \colon Z \to \CC$ expands as

$$f(x) = \sum_\xi \wh{f}(\xi)\, e_\xi(x)$$

where $\wh{f}(\xi) \in \CC$ are the <Term k="fourierCoeff">Fourier coefficients</Term>. The map $f \mapsto \wh{f}$ is the **Fourier transform**.

The characters are deliberately chosen to be "nice" — periodic exponentials, multilinear monomials, or roots of unity. Writing $f$ in terms of them means we decompose an arbitrarily complicated function into a sum of simple, symmetric pieces.

<Callout kind="remark">
The physical variable $x$ and the frequency variable $\xi$ live in the same domain $Z$ for finite groups, but play very different roles. Don't confuse them — one is the "time" side, the other is the "frequency" side.
</Callout>

<SlideLayout title="Facts about orthonormal bases">

These three identities are the engine behind every named result in Fourier analysis (Parseval, Plancherel, inversion). They are pure linear-algebra facts — no analysis needed when the space is finite-dimensional.

<Callout kind="proposition" title="Facts about orthonormal bases">
Let $V$ be a complex <Term k="hilbertSpace">Hilbert space</Term> with orthonormal basis $(e_\xi)_\xi$. Write $x = \sum_\xi a_\xi e_\xi$ and $y = \sum_\xi b_\xi e_\xi$. Then:

$$\langle x, x \rangle = \sum_\xi |a_\xi|^2$$

$$a_\xi = \langle x, e_\xi \rangle$$

$$\langle x, y \rangle = \sum_\xi a_\xi \overline{b_\xi}$$
</Callout>
